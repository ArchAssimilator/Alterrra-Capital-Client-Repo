# Executive Summary

Alterra Capital Partners is an Africa-focused private equity firm established in 2020 by the former Sub-Saharan Africa investment team of The Carlyle Group[\[1\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=THE%20ORIGIN)[\[2\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20said%20Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Eric,new%20venture%20dubbed%20Alterra%20Capital). The firm was created as Carlyle spun out its dedicated Africa fund team, allowing Alterra to assume management of Carlyle’s $700 million Sub-Saharan Africa Fund (CSSAF) and continue investing independently[\[3\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/pe-industry-news/alterra-capital-to-take-over-management-of-carlyle-sub-saharan-africa-fund/#:~:text=Carlyle%20Group%20is%20spinning%20out,700m%20regional%20fund%2C%20Bloomberg%20reported)[\[4\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20said%20it%20will%20have,advisors%20starting%20from%20July%201). Alterra’s leadership subsequently expanded by integrating senior partners from Emerging Capital Partners (ECP), combining two leading pan-African PE franchises into one platform[\[5\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,in%20Johannesburg%2C%20Nairobi%2C%20and%20Mauritius). Today Alterra is led by a seasoned team with over 100 years of African private equity experience, a track record of \~$1.9 billion invested in 20+ companies across the continent, and a multi-national presence with offices in Johannesburg, Nairobi, and Mauritius[\[6\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=The%20Alterra%20Capital%20Partners%20team,the%20growth%20of%20its%20investments)[\[7\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,services%2C%20healthcare%2C%20technology%2C%20and%20telecommunications).

Alterra’s investment strategy targets high-growth, “domestic demand” sectors serving basic consumer and business needs, with a focus on companies that can leverage technology and scalable models to become market leaders[\[8\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20Capital%20Partners%20is%20an,consumer%20and%20vital%20business%20needs)[\[9\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20seeks%20significant%20capital%20appreciation,strategy%20with%20five%20core%20elements). The firm emphasizes active portfolio management and partnership with entrepreneurs, pursuing value creation through operational improvements, strategic expansion (often via M\&A), and digital transformation[\[9\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20seeks%20significant%20capital%20appreciation,strategy%20with%20five%20core%20elements)[\[10\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=During%20the%20ownership%20period%2C%20the,markets%20including%20Kenya%20and%20Nigeria). Alterra also embeds Environmental, Social, Governance & Impact (ESG\&I) principles throughout the investment lifecycle, aligning with IFC Performance Standards and being a UN PRI signatory[\[11\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%E2%80%99s%20approach%20to%20sustainability%2C%20outlined,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation)[\[12\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20a%20signatory%20to,recommendations%20and%202X%20Challenge%20criteria). It has defined impact focus areas such as job creation, women’s economic empowerment, and climate change mitigation[\[13\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Corporation%E2%80%99s%20Environmental%20and%20Social%20Performance,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation). This dual focus on commercial growth and sustainability is reflected in Alterra’s ESG\&I Policy and its implementation across portfolio companies.

Since inception, Alterra has managed both legacy assets from Carlyle’s CSSAF fund and new investments via its inaugural independent fund, the Alterra Africa Accelerator Fund I. The new fund achieved a first close of $140 million in 2023 and is backed by prominent development finance institutions (IFC, Norfund, DEG), strategic investors (Standard Bank, Allianz’s AfricaGrow), and notable individuals including Carlyle’s co-founders David Rubenstein and Bill Conway, as well as African industrialist Aliko Dangote[\[14\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20announced%20the%20first%20close,as%20African%20industrialist%20Aliko%20Dangote). The fund targets a final cap of $500 million[\[14\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20announced%20the%20first%20close,as%20African%20industrialist%20Aliko%20Dangote), positioning Alterra among the larger Africa-focused PE vehicles. Alterra’s ability to secure such backing – including $20 million from British International Investment (CDC)[\[15\]](https://empowerafrica.com/alterra-capital-partners-secures-20-million-investment-from-british-international-investment/#:~:text=,BII) and $15 million from SIFEM (Switzerland’s DFI)[\[16\]](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthony-njoroge-42a92710_i-am-very-proud-to-share-that-sifem-has-committed-activity-7354210824548245506-ESpi#:~:text=I%20am%20very%20proud%20to,performing%20African)[\[17\]](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthony-njoroge-42a92710_i-am-very-proud-to-share-that-sifem-has-committed-activity-7354210824548245506-ESpi#:~:text=institutional%20strength%20across%20its%20portfolio,Sangudi%2C%20Bryce%20Fort%2C%20Rotondwa%20Nengovhela) – underscores investor confidence in the team’s experience and strategy.

Alterra’s current portfolio blends legacy holdings from the Carlyle fund (e.g. a Ugandan pharma distributor, a pan-African credit ratings agency, etc.) with new platform investments under its Accelerator Fund (e.g. an East African travel and tourism operator, a South African industrial vehicle manufacturer). The firm’s **“Select Team Deals”** track record includes companies across healthcare, financial services, consumer/retail, TMT, industrial, and natural resources sectors (see Portfolio Overview). Notable successes include exits like the sale of **Amrod** (promotional products supplier) to Oppenheimer Partners in 2022[\[18\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=Carlyle%20invested%20in%20Amrod%20in,manage%20CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Amrod)[\[19\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=Jacob%20Hinson%2C%20Founding%20Partner%20at,%E2%80%9D), **Amecor** (security tech) to ASSA ABLOY in 2024[\[20\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=26%20January%202024%20%E2%80%93%20Global,the%20transaction%20were%20not%20disclosed)[\[21\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Through%20its%20Carlyle%20Sub,to%20CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Amecor), and **CMC Networks** (pan-African telco) to Saudi Telecom’s subsidiary in 2023[\[22\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20is%20being%20acquired,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20CMC%20Networks)[\[23\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=Eric%20Kump%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,to%20customary%20conditions%20and%20approvals). These exits to strategic buyers highlight Alterra’s value creation and its ability to realize investments despite Africa’s often illiquid markets. At the same time, the track record also reflects challenges: for example Carlyle/Alterra’s 2014 investment in Nigeria’s Diamond Bank struggled amid macroeconomic downturn, ultimately resulting in Diamond’s merger into Access Bank by 2019[\[24\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%27s%20deals%20in%20Africa%20have,over%20Diamond%20Bank%20last%20year)[\[25\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20said%20Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Eric,new%20venture%20dubbed%20Alterra%20Capital). This mixed experience has informed Alterra’s current strategy to focus on resilient, technology-enabled businesses in essential sectors[\[8\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20Capital%20Partners%20is%20an,consumer%20and%20vital%20business%20needs)[\[9\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20seeks%20significant%20capital%20appreciation,strategy%20with%20five%20core%20elements).

**Competitive Position:** Alterra operates in a niche of mid- to large-cap African private equity, where only a handful of players have comparable scale and pan-African focus. Peers include firms like Helios, DPI, Actis, and Adenia, as well as regional funds. Many global buyout firms have pulled back from Africa due to smaller deal sizes and exit difficulties[\[26\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=This%20makes%20Carlyle%20the%20latest,can%20be%20saddled%20with%20debt), which positions Alterra (as a locally rooted team with Carlyle/ECP heritage) to fill the gap. Alterra’s competitive advantages include its deep on-ground network, seasoned team with a decade-long collaboration history, and a differentiated investor base blending global and African capital. Its main challenges lie in the inherent risks of African markets – currency volatility, political/regulatory risk, and limited exit avenues – which Alterra mitigates through rigorous due diligence, diversification across regions, and building businesses to strategic relevance.

**SWOT Analysis:** At the firm level, Alterra’s **strengths** are its experienced leadership (ex-Carlyle and ECP), strong fundraising from DFIs/strategics, and proven ability to execute deals and exits in Africa[\[27\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=Carlyle%20invested%20in%20Amrod%20in,manage%20CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Amrod)[\[28\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/carlyle-exits-south-african-company#:~:text=Through%20its%20Carlyle%20Sub,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Safety%20SA). Its pan-African presence (South, East, and representation in West Africa via partners) and sector breadth enable broad deal sourcing. Key **weaknesses** include its status as a relatively new brand (independent track record is still building) and reliance on development finance LPs which can bring constraints (e.g. ESG compliance requirements, potentially slower investment processes). The firm’s lean size means careful allocation of resources across diverse markets. **Opportunities** include the growing need for growth capital in African mid-market companies as global firms retreat[\[26\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=This%20makes%20Carlyle%20the%20latest,can%20be%20saddled%20with%20debt), attractive demographics fueling sectors like fintech, healthcare, logistics, and the chance to take over undercapitalized family businesses or divisions (corporate carve-outs) in Africa. Additionally, Alterra’s emphasis on technology-driven growth positions it well to back Africa’s emerging “industry champions” and benefit from digital leapfrogging trends[\[29\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThis%20is%20an%20excellent%20time,%E2%80%9D)[\[30\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-cobra/#:~:text=Renschke%20Volschenk%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,%E2%80%9D). **Threats** involve macroeconomic shocks (e.g. commodity price swings, currency devaluations) that can erode investment returns, plus competition from other PE funds (DPI, Helios, etc.) on quality deals. Political instability or policy changes in key countries (Nigeria, South Africa, etc.) remain a risk. Reputationally, Alterra must also navigate any legacy issues from Carlyle’s portfolio and ensure positive perceptions as an Africa-focused manager in a post-pandemic environment where LPs are cautious. So far, Alterra has managed these challenges by securing strategic exits and maintaining investor support, but continued discipline is needed to sustain momentum.

**Generative AI Masterclass Implications:** For Alterra and its portfolio companies, the rise of generative AI (GenAI) presents both promising use cases and considerations around data readiness and ethics. Many Alterra investees operate in data-rich environments – **financial services** (credit ratings, banking), **healthcare** (pharma distribution), **logistics** and **retail** – where GenAI could unlock efficiencies and new products. For example, portfolio companies could deploy AI chatbots for customer service (in banking or travel bookings), use AI-driven predictive analytics for supply chain and inventory optimization (in distribution and retail), or leverage large language models to generate insights from financial data (credit rating methodologies or loan assessments). In the **tourism sector**, Alterra’s travel operator (ARP) might use GenAI to personalize trip itineraries or automate marketing content, while in **manufacturing**, a company like Cobra could implement AI for predictive maintenance of vehicles or faster engineering design iterations. However, to realize these benefits, companies must assess their **data readiness**: many African firms face challenges with fragmented or limited datasets, legacy IT systems, and skills gaps in data science. A masterclass should therefore guide executives on evaluating their data infrastructure and partnering to fill gaps (e.g. cloud services, data engineering) before layering GenAI solutions.

Moreover, Alterra’s emphasis on ESG and impact means the **ethical and risk aspects** of AI adoption are crucial. Topics to address include data privacy compliance (especially as several African countries enact data protection laws), avoiding biases in AI models that could inadvertently disadvantage certain groups (for instance, ensuring a credit scoring AI does not reflect historical bias against underserved communities), and cybersecurity considerations when deploying AI tools. Given Alterra’s focus on women’s empowerment and job creation, the masterclass can highlight how AI can be used responsibly to augment human roles rather than displace them, and how to upskill staff for an AI-enabled workplace. The session should also cover governance: implementing AI ethically might involve creating oversight committees or guidelines within portfolio companies to monitor AI decisions, ensure transparency (important in sectors like finance/credit), and manage reputational risk. **In summary**, Alterra’s leadership and portfolio executives would benefit from tailored GenAI use cases aligned to their industries, practical steps for integrating AI into business processes (starting with pilot projects in areas like customer engagement or operations), and frameworks for mitigating risks (bias, data security, regulatory compliance) in an African context. This will prepare them to harness GenAI’s potential to drive efficiency and innovation, while upholding the developmental and ethical values central to Alterra’s mission.

## Company Profile: Alterra Capital Partners

**History & Origin:** Alterra Capital Partners was formed in mid-2020 as a spin-out of The Carlyle Group’s Sub-Saharan Africa private equity team[\[3\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/pe-industry-news/alterra-capital-to-take-over-management-of-carlyle-sub-saharan-africa-fund/#:~:text=Carlyle%20Group%20is%20spinning%20out,700m%20regional%20fund%2C%20Bloomberg%20reported). Carlyle had launched its Africa-focused fund (CSSAF) in 2012, but by 2020 decided not to raise a second Africa fund, opting instead to transition management of the $700 million CSSAF to the local team’s new independent vehicle[\[31\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20will%20remain%20active%20in,including%20Carlyle%20International%20Energy%20Partners)[\[2\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20said%20Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Eric,new%20venture%20dubbed%20Alterra%20Capital). On July 1, 2020, Alterra officially assumed sub-advisory responsibility for the remaining CSSAF portfolio, with Carlyle retaining a strategic relationship (providing co-investment and advice but no ownership stake in Alterra)[\[4\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20said%20it%20will%20have,advisors%20starting%20from%20July%201). The founding partners of Alterra included **Eric Kump**, **Genevieve Sangudi**, **Idris Mohammed**, and **Bruce Steen**, all senior dealmakers from Carlyle’s Africa fund[\[2\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20said%20Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Eric,new%20venture%20dubbed%20Alterra%20Capital). This move was part of a wider trend of global PE firms scaling back in Africa, leaving spin-offs like Alterra to carry on the mandates with more local autonomy[\[26\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=This%20makes%20Carlyle%20the%20latest,can%20be%20saddled%20with%20debt).

In 2021, Alterra’s platform expanded through the addition of two veteran investors from Emerging Capital Partners: **Bryce Fort** and **Paul Maasdorp**, who joined as Managing Directors in Alterra’s Nairobi office[\[5\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,in%20Johannesburg%2C%20Nairobi%2C%20and%20Mauritius). Fort and Maasdorp had each spent over a decade at ECP (a pioneering Africa-focused PE firm) and brought considerable deal experience, particularly in East Africa. The integration of ECP personnel effectively combined two leading pan-African PE franchises under the Alterra brand[\[5\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,in%20Johannesburg%2C%20Nairobi%2C%20and%20Mauritius). Another ECP professional, **Renschke Volschenk**, also joined Alterra’s leadership, later becoming a Managing Director in Johannesburg[\[32\]](https://alterracapital.com/our-team/#:~:text=Renschke%20Volschenk). By 2022, Alterra’s senior team comprised six Managing Directors with backgrounds at Carlyle Africa or ECP, averaging \~20 years PE experience each[\[33\]](https://alterracapital.com/our-team/#:~:text=Image)[\[34\]](https://alterracapital.com/our-team/#:~:text=Bruce%20Steen). This unique heritage is highlighted on Alterra’s website: “Alterra is an experienced pan-African fund manager formed from the combination of two leading PE franchises – Carlyle Africa and ECP”[\[5\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,in%20Johannesburg%2C%20Nairobi%2C%20and%20Mauritius).

Today, Alterra Capital Partners is headquartered in Johannesburg with additional offices in **Nairobi** and **Mauritius**[\[7\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,services%2C%20healthcare%2C%20technology%2C%20and%20telecommunications). (London is also noted as a base for one MD, Eric Kump[\[35\]](https://alterracapital.com/our-team/#:~:text=Eric%20Kump), though the firm’s official footprint is Africa-based). The Johannesburg office anchors operations for Southern Africa, Nairobi covers East Africa, and Mauritius likely serves as a fund domicile and financial hub. The team totals around \~15 investment professionals plus functional staff. Beyond the Managing Directors, Alterra’s roster includes Directors and Vice Presidents drawn from diverse backgrounds (e.g. ex-consultants, bankers, and private equity analysts) working under the partners’ guidance[\[36\]](https://alterracapital.com/our-team/#:~:text=INVESTMENT%20PROFESSIONALS)[\[37\]](https://alterracapital.com/our-team/#:~:text=Image). The firm’s **staffing scale** is lean but targeted – for instance, Alterra has a dedicated Head of ESG & Impact (Alison Kieler, formerly ECP’s ESG head)[\[38\]](https://alterracapital.com/our-team/#:~:text=Alison%20Kieler), underlining its commitment to sustainability integration.

**Industry Focus & Investment Strategy:** Alterra positions itself as a **generalist growth-capital investor** centered on Africa’s “basic consumer and vital business needs”[\[8\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20Capital%20Partners%20is%20an,consumer%20and%20vital%20business%20needs). In practice, the firm focuses on sectors that cater to the continent’s growing middle class and enterprise infrastructure. Key target industries include **consumer goods and retail**, **healthcare and pharmaceuticals**, **financial services**, **telecommunications & technology (TMT)**, **business services**, **industrial/manufacturing**, and select **natural resources** opportunities[\[8\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20Capital%20Partners%20is%20an,consumer%20and%20vital%20business%20needs)[\[9\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20seeks%20significant%20capital%20appreciation,strategy%20with%20five%20core%20elements). Alterra prefers **“domestic-facing” companies** – those whose demand is driven by local/regional consumption rather than purely export markets[\[8\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20Capital%20Partners%20is%20an,consumer%20and%20vital%20business%20needs). This aligns with Africa’s demographic trends and the need for services like banking, healthcare, food supply, logistics, etc., to serve local populations.

A core element of Alterra’s strategy is leveraging technology to **transform business models and drive scale**[\[39\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Image%3A%20alterra). Even in traditional sectors (agriculture, logistics, manufacturing), the team looks for companies that can adopt tech-enabled efficiencies or digital distribution. For example, Alterra highlights identifying companies with **scalable models that can become market leaders** in their niche by accelerating growth (often through regional expansion or bolt-on acquisitions)[\[40\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Create%20market%20leaders%20by%20identifying,companies%20with%20scalable%20business%20models). The firm typically seeks **control or significant minority stakes** in its investees, often injecting expansion capital and taking board seats to actively influence strategy[\[30\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-cobra/#:~:text=Renschke%20Volschenk%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,%E2%80%9D)[\[41\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=During%20the%20ownership%20period%2C%20the,markets%20including%20Kenya%20and%20Nigeria). According to public statements, Alterra pursues **active portfolio management and engagement** with partners as one of five core strategy pillars[\[42\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Image%3A%20alterra). This hands-on approach was evident in how Alterra (as Carlyle’s sub-advisor) worked with portfolio companies: e.g., at Amrod they helped execute an M\&A strategy and navigate the COVID-19 period[\[41\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=During%20the%20ownership%20period%2C%20the,markets%20including%20Kenya%20and%20Nigeria), and at Amecor they refocused the business on recurring network services revenue[\[43\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=During%20Carlyle%E2%80%99s%20ownership%20period%2C%20Alterra,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market)[\[44\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=to%20shift%20the%20company%E2%80%99s%20focus,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market).

Alterra’s **investment philosophy** emphasizes “significant capital appreciation” through growth rather than high leverage[\[9\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20seeks%20significant%20capital%20appreciation,strategy%20with%20five%20core%20elements). African deals often involve minority equity and structured equity more than heavy debt, given relatively underdeveloped leverage markets. Thus, Alterra’s value creation is driven by operational improvements: strengthening management teams, upgrading systems, expanding into new markets, and sometimes consolidating industries. The team’s long Africa tenure has given them experience with both entrepreneurial family businesses and corporate carve-outs. Notably, several of Alterra’s deals were family-owned firms where the PE investment aided succession and expansion (e.g., Abacus in healthcare was founder-led, Amrod was founder-led, etc.). In summary, Alterra’s strategy is to back **established mid-market African companies** that are leaders or potential leaders in essential sectors, infuse capital and expertise to turbocharge their growth (organically and via acquisitions), and ultimately exit to strategic buyers or via public markets if feasible.

## Investment Strategy and Value Creation

Alterra’s investment strategy can be distilled into **five core elements** the firm highlights, aimed at generating outsized capital appreciation while maintaining an impact lens[\[9\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20seeks%20significant%20capital%20appreciation,strategy%20with%20five%20core%20elements)[\[42\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Image%3A%20alterra):

1. **Target Essential, Domestic-Facing Businesses:** Alterra looks for companies that service fundamental needs in their local economies – such as healthcare, food supply chains, financial inclusion, communications, and basic consumer goods[\[8\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%20Capital%20Partners%20is%20an,consumer%20and%20vital%20business%20needs). By focusing on these sectors, Alterra taps into resilient demand driven by Africa’s growing population and rising consumption. For example, Alterra (through Carlyle) invested in **Abacus Pharmaceuticals**, a Ugandan drug manufacturer/distributor, recognizing the vital role of affordable medicines in East Africa[\[56\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=Johannesburg%2C%20South%20Africa%2C%2013%20September,Financial%20terms%20are%20not%20disclosed)[\[57\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=chronic%20therapeutic%20areas%20such%20as,quality%20and%20extensive%20distribution%20network). Similarly, it backed **Export Trading Group (ETG)**, an agricultural supply chain integrator, given the importance of food staples and agro-commodities in Africa (ETG connects small farmers to global markets). These investments serve basic needs (health and food) and thus benefit from secular growth trends, insulating them somewhat from cyclical swings.

2. **Leverage Technology for Scale:** Alterra explicitly emphasizes leveraging technology to transform and scale business models[\[39\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Image%3A%20alterra). In practice, this means the firm favors companies that either are tech-enabled or have clear avenues to become so. For instance, in the **financial services** realm, Alterra’s team invested in a Nigerian online travel agency (**Wakanow**) which leverages the internet to reach customers regionally[\[58\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=). In **communications infrastructure**, **CMC Networks** (one of their deals) provided connectivity solutions across Africa[\[59\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Founded%20in%201989%2C%20CMC%20Networks,carriers%20serving%20end%20customers%20in%E2%80%A6); under Alterra’s guidance, CMC likely invested in network upgrades and software-defined networking to improve efficiency before its eventual sale[\[60\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20operates%20across%20more,the%20Americas%2C%20and%20Asia%20Pacific)[\[22\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20is%20being%20acquired,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20CMC%20Networks). Alterra’s new investment in **Cobra Group** (a manufacturer of specialized vehicles) also involves technology: Cobra is integrating data and tech to drive productivity and safety in its equipment[\[61\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-cobra/#:~:text=Commenting%20on%20the%20partnership%2C%20Lloyd,%E2%80%9D)[\[62\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-cobra/#:~:text=The%20company%E2%80%99s%20growth%20strategy%20includes,to%20support%20Cobra%E2%80%99s%20future%20initiatives). By infusing or enhancing technology in traditionally analog sectors (like manufacturing or logistics), Alterra aims to create **operational leverage** – enabling companies to expand output without commensurate increases in cost, thus boosting margins and valuations.

3. **Build Market Leaders via Accelerated Growth:** Alterra seeks companies with a **scalable model and regional expansion potential**, then helps accelerate their growth to achieve market leadership[\[40\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Create%20market%20leaders%20by%20identifying,companies%20with%20scalable%20business%20models). A prime example is **TiAuto**, a South African tire retail and wholesale company. Carlyle’s Africa team (now Alterra) acquired TiAuto around 2014 with a thesis to expand its retail footprint and brand portfolio. Another example, **J\&J Africa (Transport)**, was a Mozambican logistics firm that Carlyle/Alterra grew into one of the largest trucking and logistics operators on the Beira corridor[\[63\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Sector%3A%20Industrial). Over the investment period, J\&J expanded routes and capacity, making it attractive enough that Imperial Logistics acquired it in 2021 for $300 million, giving Alterra (and co-investor Ninety One) a full exit[\[64\]](https://www.globalprivatecapital.org/newsroom/imperial-logistics-to-acquire-carlyle-and-ninety-one-backed-jj-africa/#:~:text=Imperial%20Logistics%20agreed%20to%20acquire,Read%20more)[\[65\]](https://www.globalprivatecapital.org/newsroom/imperial-logistics-to-acquire-carlyle-and-ninety-one-backed-jj-africa/#:~:text=INDUSTRY%20NEWS). These cases illustrate Alterra’s playbook: identify a strong platform, inject growth capital and expertise, scale it into the regional leader, and then exit (often to a strategic who values the established market position). The firm has also pursued **buy-and-build strategies** – for instance, at Amrod (promotional products) they supported acquisitions and new market entries (Kenya, Nigeria) to broaden its Africa presence[\[41\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=During%20the%20ownership%20period%2C%20the,markets%20including%20Kenya%20and%20Nigeria). Alterra’s partners often take **board seats and even interim management roles** where needed to drive these expansions.

4. **Active Ownership and Partnership:** Unlike passive investors, Alterra practices **hands-on portfolio management**. The team works closely with management of investee companies on strategy, operations, and governance[\[42\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Image%3A%20alterra). For example, after Carlyle’s 2019 investment in **Amecor (Safety & Security tech)**, Alterra’s Bruce Steen spent significant time with Amecor’s team to shift the business model toward recurring service revenue[\[43\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=During%20Carlyle%E2%80%99s%20ownership%20period%2C%20Alterra,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market)[\[44\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=to%20shift%20the%20company%E2%80%99s%20focus,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market). Over four years, this strategic pivot more than doubled Amecor’s subscriber base and raised recurring revenues to 70% of sales, greatly increasing its attractiveness, which culminated in a sale to ASSA ABLOY in 2024[\[43\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=During%20Carlyle%E2%80%99s%20ownership%20period%2C%20Alterra,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market)[\[66\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=subscriber%20base%20to%20over%20600%2C000,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market). Alterra often brings in external expertise (consultants or industry specialists) to support value creation plans – e.g., McKinsey was engaged during Carlyle’s Abacus deal for commercial due diligence[\[67\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=65%20transactions%20across%20the%20global,as%20of%20June%2030%2C%202018) and likely post-investment strategy support. The firm also emphasizes **management team development**: it may help recruit key executives (CFOs, COOs) or install proper financial controls in founder-led businesses. Alterra’s involvement is collaborative; their portfolio CEOs have often publicly thanked Alterra for strategic advice and “unwavering support” (as Amrod’s CEO did)[\[68\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=on%20its%20significant%20success%20to,%E2%80%9D)[\[69\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=Amit%20Brill%2C%20co,%E2%80%9D). This active engagement aligns incentives and helps implement necessary changes that smaller African firms might not execute alone.

5. **Embed ESG & Impact for Sustainable Growth:** Alterra’s strategy integrates ESG risk management and intentional impact objectives at every stage[\[11\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%E2%80%99s%20approach%20to%20sustainability%2C%20outlined,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation)[\[70\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=intentional%20plans%20to%20drive%20economic,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation). The firm’s **ESG & Impact (ESG\&I) Policy** draws on IFC Performance Standards to ensure environmental and social risks are identified and mitigated in due diligence[\[11\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Alterra%E2%80%99s%20approach%20to%20sustainability%2C%20outlined,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation). For instance, before investing in industrial or natural resource deals like **Assala Energy (oil production)**, the team would assess environmental compliance and community impact, aligning with IFC standards (indeed, IFC co-invested alongside Carlyle in some deals, requiring these standards). Beyond risk mitigation, Alterra pursues positive impact: it prioritizes sectors and companies that contribute to job creation, skills development, women’s empowerment, and climate adaptation[\[70\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=intentional%20plans%20to%20drive%20economic,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation). Examples include **NOSA (Safety SA)** – whose services inherently improve workplace health and safety across industries, and **ARP Travel Group**, which Alterra supports as a driver of sustainable tourism and local employment in East Africa[\[71\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/deals/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-arp-africa/#:~:text=Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,%E2%80%9D)[\[72\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/deals/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-arp-africa/#:~:text=ARP%20Africa%2C%20which%20includes%20Pollman%E2%80%99s,management%20services%20in%20East%20Africa). Alterra tracks impact metrics (they align with frameworks like the Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures and the 2X Challenge for gender inclusion)[\[73\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=women%E2%80%99s%20empowerment%2C%20and%20climate%20change,mitigation%20and%20adaptation). In practice, this could mean monitoring how many jobs are created or how many women-led SMEs are supported through an investee’s activities. By embedding ESG\&I, Alterra not only upholds their developmental mandate (important for DFI LPs) but also **creates long-term value** – companies with strong ESG practices are more resilient and attractive to strategic acquirers. Indeed, Alterra’s exits often underscore these points: the sale of **Safety SA (NOSA)** to a global strategic (CTI) was facilitated by showing the company’s robust safety impact and growth under Alterra’s stewardship[\[74\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/carlyle-exits-south-african-company#:~:text=Founded%20in%201952%20as%20NOSA%2C,of%20over%20540%20skilled%20professionals)[\[28\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/carlyle-exits-south-african-company#:~:text=Through%20its%20Carlyle%20Sub,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Safety%20SA).

Underpinning these strategic elements, Alterra typically **invests in the range of \~$20–$60 million per deal**, though smaller bolt-ons can be financed through follow-ons. The holding period tends to be **4–6 years**, flexible to market conditions. The **fund’s geographic diversification** is another strategy aspect: by spreading investments across Africa’s major economies (South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, etc.), Alterra balances localized risks (currency, regulation). Their prior fund CSSAF had investments in at least 8 countries[\[75\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/pe-industry-news/alterra-capital-to-take-over-management-of-carlyle-sub-saharan-africa-fund/#:~:text=Launched%20in%202011%2C%20this%20fund,Mozambique%2C%20Botswana%2C%20Zambia%20and%20Uganda), and the new fund likewise aims for a pan-African mix.

Alterra’s **value-creation approach** has already yielded tangible results: e.g., in Amrod’s case, Alterra helped improve operations and navigate the pandemic, which contributed to a successful exit at an undisclosed but reportedly strong return[\[76\]](https://www.rmb.co.za/news/rmb-advises-on-sale-of-amrod#:~:text=RMB%20advises%20on%20sale%20of,are%20managed%20by%20Alterra)[\[77\]](https://www.rmb.co.za/news/rmb-advises-on-sale-of-amrod#:~:text=The%20sale%20of%20a%20majority,are%20managed%20by%20Alterra). Conversely, the firm has learned from less successful outcomes (like Diamond Bank) the importance of factors such as prudent financial structuring and not over-relying on macro-growth without sufficient buffers for downturns[\[78\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%27s%20deals%20in%20Africa%20have,over%20Diamond%20Bank%20last%20year)[\[79\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Bank%2C%20which%20soured%20after%20a,over%20Diamond%20Bank%20last%20year). As a result, Alterra now often seeks majority or controlling minority stakes where it can enact changes quickly, and favors companies with some resilience to external shocks (basic needs sectors, as noted).

In summary, Alterra’s investment strategy is a **blend of growth equity and buyout principles** adapted to Africa: take significant stakes in essential-service companies, use technology and expertise to scale them into market leaders, manage actively, and do so with a keen eye on ESG impacts for durable success. This strategy not only aims for high financial returns but also aligns with the development agendas of the firm’s investors and the broader growth story of the African continent.

Notably, since Alterra took over, they have executed exits for Amrod (2022), CMC Networks (2023), Amecor (2024), and agreed exit for NOSA/Safety SA (2025)[\[74\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/carlyle-exits-south-african-company#:~:text=Founded%20in%201952%20as%20NOSA%2C,of%20over%20540%20skilled%20professionals)[\[20\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=26%20January%202024%20%E2%80%93%20Global,the%20transaction%20were%20not%20disclosed). ## Competitive Landscape

Alterra Capital Partners operates in the context of **Africa-focused private equity** where the competitive landscape includes both regional generalist funds and sector-specific or country-specific players. Below is an overview of its peers and how Alterra differentiates itself:

**Peer Set – Pan-African Generalist Funds:** The closest competitors to Alterra are other mid- to large-cap pan-African PE firms:

* **Development Partners International (DPI):** A London-based Africa specialist with \~$2.5 billion across three funds (their third fund closed at $900m in 2021). DPI, like Alterra, invests broadly in growth companies across Africa. They have a similar DFI-heavy LP base and focus on the emerging middle-class theme. DPI’s scale is somewhat larger; however, Alterra’s team has comparable experience. Both emphasize impact and ESG (DPI has an extra focus on gender-lens investing via the 2X Challenge, which Alterra also aligns with[\[73\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=women%E2%80%99s%20empowerment%2C%20and%20climate%20change,mitigation%20and%20adaptation)). DPI is a strong competitor on deals in tech, financial services, and other sectors, but Alterra might differentiate by the combined Carlyle/ECP network and by flexibility to do both buyouts and growth minority stakes.

* **Helios Investment Partners:** Helios is one of Africa’s largest indigenous PE firms, historically raising \~$1 billion funds. They focus on bigger transactions and have done several landmark deals (e.g., telecom towers, payments). Helios’s Fund IV has faced a challenging raise, but they remain a key competitor especially for Nigerian or West African opportunities. Compared to Helios, Alterra is slightly smaller and more DFI-backed, whereas Helios had more commercial LPs. Alterra might collaborate or co-invest with Helios on very large deals, but more often they’ll compete in sectors like financial services or energy where both have interest. A differentiator is Helios tends to do more control and occasionally infrastructure-heavy deals, whereas Alterra (with ECP influence) might be more flexible and mid-market oriented.

* **Actis:** Actis is a global emerging markets investor with a significant Africa portfolio (spun out of CDC). They manage sector-specific funds in energy and real estate, and general funds historically. Actis’s newest Africa fund initiatives are a bit segmented. While Actis is a heavyweight, they often pursue infrastructure or big platform deals (like power generation, or acquiring banks) that Alterra may not directly target. Still, Actis has gone after some consumer and industrial assets too. Actis’s scale dwarfs Alterra’s (Actis has multi-billion AUM), but Actis has partly shifted focus to broader emerging Asia as well. Alterra can capitalize on being Africa-pure-play whereas Actis is diversifying.

* **Adenia Partners:** A mid-market PE fund focusing on Franco- and Anglophone Africa, with fund sizes in the $300m range (Adenia Fund V closed at $300m in 2022). Adenia often targets similar mid-sized companies and also integrates ESG. They are peers in deals particularly in East Africa or islands (Adenia has Indian Ocean roots). Alterra’s advantage might be its higher profile deals and multi-origin team; Adenia has a solid track record but is perhaps more conservative in sector spread. They could compete on deals like manufacturing or agribusiness companies in East/Southern Africa.

* **Emerging Capital Partners (ECP):** It is worth noting ECP itself (the firm from which some Alterra partners came) still exists and has been raising a new fund (though slowly). ECP historically did deals across Africa (telecom, financial, infrastructure). With Fort, Maasdorp, Volschenk now at Alterra, ECP’s presence in East/Southern Africa is diminished, but ECP still has West Africa strength (with other partners). So on West African deals (e.g., Ivorian, Nigerian), ECP could be a competitor or even co-investor. However, ECP’s ability to raise large capital has been challenged; they last closed a major fund in 2010\. Alterra in a way supplanted ECP in the eyes of some DFIs as the “new generation” team to back[\[5\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,in%20Johannesburg%2C%20Nairobi%2C%20and%20Mauritius).

* **Ethos Private Equity:** Based in South Africa, Ethos was historically the leading SA-focused PE firm (funds \~$500m–800m size). They focus mainly on South Africa and some regional expansion plays. On purely South African opportunities, Ethos and Alterra might both pursue the same assets (for instance, Ethos invests in similar sectors like industrials, services, and had a fund for mid-market). Alterra’s edge is a broader Africa mandate and international LP backing, whereas Ethos has more local institutional backing. Ethos recently has faced internal changes (merging with AIIM on some platform), so Alterra could have an opening to claim pan-African deals Ethos might have once chased.

**Sector or Regional Competitors:** Depending on the deal, Alterra faces competition from specialists:

* For **tech-enabled or fintech deals**, specialist VC/growth funds like Partech Africa, TLcom, or Novastar might be in the mix (though those usually do smaller ticket sizes). Alterra’s sweet spot is slightly later-stage than pure venture, so they may come in when companies are scaling (Series C+ stage), where some of these VC funds might co-invest or hand off.

* In **infrastructure or energy deals**, players like African Infrastructure Investment Managers (AIIM) or Harith might compete if the asset has infra characteristics. For example, if Alterra looked at telecom towers or energy assets, they’d face these infra funds. But Alterra seems more focused on corporate operating companies rather than project finance.

* In **East Africa** deals, firms like Ascent Capital (mid-market East Africa), Fanisi, or TPG’s Rise Fund (for impact deals) might show up. Alterra’s Fort and Maasdorp give it a strong East Africa footing, possibly stronger than say DPI’s local presence, which could be a competitive advantage.

* **Nigerian market:** Helios and Sahel Capital, and some indigenous funds (Verod, African Capital Alliance) are active. Alterra may selectively compete there (e.g., if they consider fintech or consumer in Nigeria, they’ll meet ACA or Verod). But Alterra’s track record includes Nigeria (Diamond Bank, Wakanow) so they have experience, even if cautious post-Diamond.

**Differentiation:** Alterra differentiates itself in a few key ways. First, its **hybrid heritage** (Carlyle \+ ECP) means it combines global institutional rigor with on-ground African entrepreneurship experience[\[5\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=Alterra%20is%20an%20experienced%20pan,in%20Johannesburg%2C%20Nairobi%2C%20and%20Mauritius). This is seen as a plus by LPs (hence the support from DFIs and prominent investors). Second, Alterra’s explicit **ESG\&I integration** and developmental mandate stands out. Many competitors also tout ESG, but Alterra’s UN PRI signatory status and detailed ESG policy (viewable on their site) show a commitment that resonates especially with European investors[\[70\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=intentional%20plans%20to%20drive%20economic,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation). This could give them an edge in winning DFI money or in managing sensitive assets (e.g., they can handle environmental/social issues competently, which not all funds do equally).

Third, Alterra’s **strategic relationships** can be leveraged: Carlyle, while not an LP, remains a partner for co-investments and advice[\[4\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20said%20it%20will%20have,advisors%20starting%20from%20July%201). This means if Alterra finds a very large deal, Carlyle or its funds might co-invest (e.g., Carlyle’s global funds could join on an African deal outside the accelerator fund’s scope). Also, having Dangote involved could open doors in industries across Africa, given Dangote Group’s footprint (cement, refining, etc.). Few peers have that kind of high-profile African business leader support.

Additionally, Alterra might emphasize a **flexible deal structuring** approach – willing to do majority or minority, public-to-private deals, and so on. Some competitors have narrower mandates (e.g., a fund might only do control deals, or only certain countries). Alterra’s track record shows they’ve done everything from minority stakes in banks to full buyouts of companies like Amrod or Amecor[\[21\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Through%20its%20Carlyle%20Sub,to%20CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Amecor)[\[93\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=%24147%20million%20investment%20sees%20The,Group%20become%20a%20significant%20shareholder). This flexibility can be a selling point to company owners who may want a tailor-made deal.

**Competitive Challenges:** That said, Alterra is also a newcomer brand – it will need to prove itself against incumbents like DPI and Helios, especially when fundraising from non-DFI capital. Some large U.S. or Middle East investors might prefer to invest with a Helios (with longer history) or an Actis for lower perceived risk. Alterra’s heavy DFI backing is double-edged: it assures a base of capital but could signal that pure commercial LPs are less involved so far. To break out, Alterra might want to attract some pension funds or sovereign funds beyond the development institutions in future closes. They have made a start with Standard Bank and Dangote, which is encouraging[\[83\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-has-announced-the-first-close-for-the-alterra-africa-accelerator-fund-aaa-fund/#:~:text=targeted%20fund%20cap%20of%20%24500,as%20African%20industrialist%20Aliko%20Dangote).

Within Africa, **local currency risk** and exit pathways are concerns all these competitors share. Alterra’s approach to mitigate these (invest in export-hedged businesses where possible, target strategic buyers for exits) is broadly in line with peers.

**Recent Trends:** The Reuters piece in 2020 noted that global PE giants had scaled back in Africa (KKR, Blackstone exited their Africa initiatives)[\[94\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%20also%20bought%20into%20J%26J,trades%20commodities%20and%20provides%20logistics). This leaves firms like Alterra as key stewards of private capital in the region. Alterra has an opportunity to capture deals that would have gone to arms of those global firms in the past. For example, if a multinational wants to sell an African subsidiary, Alterra could be a buyer where Carlyle or KKR might previously have looked. An example is the *auto sector deal* that VCCircle hinted Alterra was pursuing (possibly a club deal for an auto parts firm, which they reportedly changed plans on)[\[95\]](https://www.vccircle.com/carlyles-africa-spinout-alterra-changes-plan-for-auto-co-buy-drops-pe-club-deal-#:~:text=Image%3A%20Carlyle%27s%20Africa%20spinout%20Alterra,firm%2C%20drops%20PE%20club%20deal) – such deals often come from multinationals divesting African units. Alterra being on the ground can react faster than non-dedicated global funds.

In summary, Alterra’s competitive landscape features a handful of pan-African PE firms (DPI, Helios, Actis, Ethos, ECP) and numerous niche players. Alterra distinguishes itself through its top-tier team with multi-decade Africa experience, a strong commitment to ESG/impact, and a demonstrated ability to navigate complex deals and exits (e.g., selling to international strategics in South Africa, facilitating management buyouts as in Traxys’s case[\[96\]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-06/carlyle-to-sell-metals-trader-traxys-to-group-led-by-high-frequency-firm-optiver#:~:text=Carlyle%20to%20Sell%20Metals%20Trader,the%20first%20time%20since), etc.). Its challenges will be to maintain deal flow and performance in a tough environment, but its recent moves suggest a growing stature. As Africa’s PE industry matures, Alterra is poised to be among the leading **“next-generation” African private equity firms** bridging global capital and local growth opportunities.

## Detailed Investee Company Profiles

### Abacus Pharma (Abacus Health Care Ltd)

**Profile:** Abacus is a pharmaceutical manufacturer and distributor based in Uganda, with operations across East Africa. Established in 1995, it has grown into one of the region’s largest suppliers of prescription and OTC drugs[\[97\]](https://alterracapital.com/abacus/#:~:text=Established%20in%201995%2C%20Abacus%20is,Tanzania%2C%20Kenya%2C%20Rwanda%20and%20Burundi). Abacus imports and distributes over 600 pharmaceutical products and is notably East Africa’s largest producer of parenteral products (IV fluids, ear/eye drops, etc.)[\[97\]](https://alterracapital.com/abacus/#:~:text=Established%20in%201995%2C%20Abacus%20is,Tanzania%2C%20Kenya%2C%20Rwanda%20and%20Burundi)[\[98\]](https://alterracapital.com/abacus/#:~:text=pharmaceuticals,Tanzania%2C%20Kenya%2C%20Rwanda%20and%20Burundi). The company operates manufacturing facilities in Uganda and distribution networks spanning Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda, and Burundi, employing over 1,000 people[\[122\]](https://alterracapital.com/abacus/#:~:text=pharmaceuticals,Tanzania%2C%20Kenya%2C%20Rwanda%20and%20Burundi)[\[123\]](https://alterracapital.com/abacus/#:~:text=largest%20manufacturer%20of%20parenteral%20pharmaceutical,Tanzania%2C%20Kenya%2C%20Rwanda%20and%20Burundi). Abacus’s product range includes critical care items like IV drips and oncology drugs as well as generic medications for common ailments. Its distribution arm reaches both urban and remote areas through 30+ wholesale branches (as of 2018\)[\[57\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=chronic%20therapeutic%20areas%20such%20as,quality%20and%20extensive%20distribution%20network)[\[124\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=has%20an%20extensive%20distribution%20network,quality%20and%20extensive%20distribution%20network). This extensive footprint and “last mile” reach make Abacus a key player in improving healthcare access in East Africa.

**Investment & Alterra’s Role:** The Carlyle Sub-Saharan Africa Fund acquired a **majority stake in Abacus** (then referred to as AK Life Sciences) in September 2018[\[56\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=Johannesburg%2C%20South%20Africa%2C%2013%20September,Financial%20terms%20are%20not%20disclosed). This investment – one of Carlyle Africa’s last before the spin-out – was driven by the thesis of rising demand for quality healthcare products in Africa. Genevieve Sangudi, Carlyle/Alterra MD, noted Abacus had “best-in-class distribution” and a platform to leverage favorable demographics and growing pharma demand[\[125\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Managing%20Director%20at,next%20phase%20of%20the%20company%E2%80%99s)[\[126\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=best,%E2%80%9D). Equity for the deal came entirely from CSSAF[\[56\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=Johannesburg%2C%20South%20Africa%2C%2013%20September,Financial%20terms%20are%20not%20disclosed). After forming Alterra in 2020, the team continued to oversee Abacus. Alterra has worked with Abacus’s founders (Ramesh Babu and family) on growth initiatives, such as broadening supplier relationships with international pharma firms and expanding into new markets[\[126\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=best,%E2%80%9D)[\[127\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=pharmaceutical%20demand%20throughout%20East%20Africa,next%20phase%20of%20the%20company%E2%80%99s). In practice, this likely meant helping Abacus secure licensing deals to distribute more multinational pharmaceutical brands and possibly evaluating expansion into West Africa or new product lines. IFC also financed Abacus’s expansion post-investment (providing a loan for equipment upgrades)[\[128\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/ifc-proposes-funding-for-carlyle-backed-ugandan-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=IFC%20proposes%20funding%20for%20Carlyle,manufacturing%20arm%20of%20the%20group), indicating alignment with development finance to scale production capacity[\[128\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/ifc-proposes-funding-for-carlyle-backed-ugandan-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=IFC%20proposes%20funding%20for%20Carlyle,manufacturing%20arm%20of%20the%20group).

**Current Status:** Abacus remains in Alterra’s legacy portfolio (no exit yet). It is considered a **current holding** under CSSAF/Alterra management. No public mention of exit plans has surfaced, implying Alterra may be waiting to further grow the company or find a strategic buyer. Abacus’s performance likely benefited from increased healthcare spending; even during COVID-19, demand for IV fluids and essential meds would have been robust. A potential exit route could be a sale to a larger pharmaceutical conglomerate or an African healthcare conglomerate. Until then, Alterra will aim to continue value creation, such as increasing Abacus’s manufacturing output and perhaps moving into higher-margin products. Abacus is strategically important as it addresses import substitution (producing locally what might otherwise be imported) – this could attract impact-oriented acquirers or IPO interest down the line.

**Competition & Market:** Abacus competes with other regional pharma manufacturers (like Kenya’s Laborex or Uganda’s Cipla-Quality Chemicals) and a plethora of importers. Its competitive advantage lies in its dual role as manufacturer-distributor and its distribution reach in remote areas[\[57\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=chronic%20therapeutic%20areas%20such%20as,quality%20and%20extensive%20distribution%20network)[\[122\]](https://alterracapital.com/abacus/#:~:text=pharmaceuticals,Tanzania%2C%20Kenya%2C%20Rwanda%20and%20Burundi). Being a local producer gives it cost and supply reliability advantages, especially for bulky IV fluids. The East African pharma market is growing, but also faces regulatory hurdles and trust issues with imports, which favors reputable local players like Abacus.

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* Strong distribution network across East Africa[\[57\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=chronic%20therapeutic%20areas%20such%20as,quality%20and%20extensive%20distribution%20network); local manufacturing capacity for essential medical products; established relationships with both multinational suppliers and local pharmacies/hospitals. Demographic tailwinds (growing population) drive demand.  
\- *Weaknesses:* Exposure to currency fluctuations (importing raw materials); needs constant regulatory compliance (drug quality standards). Production of commoditized generics can have thin margins unless scaled.  
\- *Opportunities:* Expand product portfolio (e.g., more specialized drugs like oncology treatments as hinted by Carlyle)[\[129\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-completes-acquisition-abacus-pharmaceutical-company#:~:text=ear%2C%20eye%20and%20nose%20drops,quality%20and%20extensive%20distribution%20network); potential to export to neighboring regions; partnerships with government health programs for supply contracts. Possibly attract equity from strategic pharma investors or DFIs for further expansion.  
\- *Threats:* Competition from low-cost Asian imports or new local entrants (e.g., India/China imported generics often undercut pricing); regulatory changes or price controls in countries like Kenya or Uganda could affect profitability; any quality control issues could damage reputation in a sensitive industry.

Alterra’s handling of Abacus so far appears to be one of steady scaling. Given its impact angle (healthcare access) and growth, Abacus is a likely candidate for Alterra to showcase in terms of both financial success and social good when they eventually exit.

### Access Bank (via Diamond Bank investment)

**Profile:** Access Bank is one of Nigeria’s largest commercial banks, with a pan-African footprint and international presence. The bank operates over 600 branches across 12 countries and three continents[\[130\]](https://alterracapital.com/access-bank/#:~:text=Access%20Bank%20is%20a%20leading,Bank%20has%20over%20800%2C000%20shareholders), serving retail, SME, and corporate clients. It is classified as a Systemically Important Bank by Nigeria’s central bank[\[131\]](https://alterracapital.com/access-bank/#:~:text=Sub%20Saharan%20Africa%20and%20the,Nigerian%20and%20International%20Institutional%20Investors). Access Bank as it exists today grew significantly by acquiring Diamond Bank in 2019 – a merger that created what is now one of Africa’s biggest banking groups by assets[\[132\]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-12-17/nigeria-s-access-bank-to-take-over-carlyle-backed-diamond-bank#:~:text=Nigeria%E2%80%99s%20Access%20Bank%20Plc%20,Both%20companies%E2%80%99%20shares%20rose). Access is listed on the Nigerian Stock Exchange and, as of mid-2010s, had assets over $10 billion and a strong retail franchise[\[133\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=Diamond%20Bank%20,Saharan)[\[134\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=financial%20services%20and%20products%20throughout,of%20eight%20Systemically%20Important%20Financial). The Alterra team’s connection to Access comes via **Diamond Bank Plc**, which was a mid-tier Nigerian bank they invested in. Diamond Bank, prior to merger, was known for retail banking innovation and was also designated systemically important in Nigeria[\[133\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=Diamond%20Bank%20,Saharan). Diamond’s financial struggles in 2016-2018 led to its takeover by Access Bank.

**Investment & Outcome:** In **November 2014, Carlyle (CSSAF) invested $147 million in Diamond Bank** for roughly a 17.7% stake[\[135\]\[136\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/finance/nigerias-access-bank-to-buy-rival-diamond-bank-to-create-africas-biggest-bank-idUSL8N1YM62W/#:~:text=,147%20million%20in%202014). This was done through Diamond’s NGN 50 billion rights issue (capital raise) to strengthen the bank’s balance sheet[\[93\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=%24147%20million%20investment%20sees%20The,Group%20become%20a%20significant%20shareholder). At the time, Diamond was one of Nigeria’s top retail banks, and Carlyle’s West Africa lead (Genevieve Sangudi) joined the board, supporting Diamond’s Tier-1 capital growth and IT infrastructure investments[\[137\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Managing%20Director%20and,successful%20Tier%201%20banks%20in)[\[138\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=Sub,to%20supporting%20the%20Bank%20towards). Unfortunately, Nigeria’s macro environment turned shortly after: the 2015 oil price crash, Naira devaluation, and recession hit banks hard. Diamond Bank’s non-performing loans spiked and it struggled with capital adequacy. By late 2018, Diamond Bank’s condition forced a merger with Access Bank at a low valuation (Access paid roughly $200m for the entire Diamond Bank)[\[132\]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-12-17/nigeria-s-access-bank-to-take-over-carlyle-backed-diamond-bank#:~:text=Nigeria%E2%80%99s%20Access%20Bank%20Plc%20,Both%20companies%E2%80%99%20shares%20rose)[\[139\]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-12-17/nigeria-s-access-bank-to-take-over-carlyle-backed-diamond-bank#:~:text=nation%E2%80%99s%20biggest%20lender%20by%20assets,Both%20companies%E2%80%99%20shares%20rose). Given Carlyle’s \~$147m cost, the implied exit value was significantly lower – Reuters noted Carlyle’s bet “soured” after currency and economic issues[\[78\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%27s%20deals%20in%20Africa%20have,over%20Diamond%20Bank%20last%20year).

In the **Access–Diamond merger (2019)**, Carlyle’s Diamond shares were exchanged for Access Bank shares and some cash. Access paid N3.13 per Diamond share (more than triple Diamond’s depressed market price, to incentivize the deal)[\[139\]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-12-17/nigeria-s-access-bank-to-take-over-carlyle-backed-diamond-bank#:~:text=nation%E2%80%99s%20biggest%20lender%20by%20assets,Both%20companies%E2%80%99%20shares%20rose). Even so, Carlyle likely realized a loss or at best broke even in Naira terms (in USD terms, likely a loss due to devaluation). The merged entity, Access Bank, benefited from Diamond’s customer base and has since grown to be a leading bank in Nigeria and expanding in Africa (they’ve acquired banks in Kenya, Mozambique, etc.). Carlyle/Alterra no longer holds a stake post-merger (they would have likely sold or distributed the Access Bank shares soon after). Thus, **Alterra’s involvement effectively ended in 2019** with the exit from Diamond via the Access transaction. The **status** of this investment is **Exited** (though not a positive outcome financially).

**Alterra’s Learnings:** This was a high-profile deal for Carlyle that did not go as planned. It highlighted the risks of macroeconomic exposure – Diamond’s fundamentals were strong in retail banking, but systemic shocks undermined it[\[78\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%27s%20deals%20in%20Africa%20have,over%20Diamond%20Bank%20last%20year). Alterra’s team likely learned to be more cautious on pure financial services plays that are highly tied to commodity cycles. It’s notable that in Alterra’s new fund, they have not (so far) publicly done a bank deal; instead they have a more diversified approach in financial services (e.g., credit ratings agency GCR which has pan-African revenue and less credit risk).

**Access Bank Today:** Access Bank itself is performing relatively well post-merger, and has become a buyer of other banks across Africa. While Alterra has no stake, the Diamond Bank episode is part of their track record. The team can still point to the strategic insight of backing a top Nigerian bank, even if timing was unlucky. And indeed, Access Bank’s success in creating “Africa’s largest bank” by some metrics validates part of the original thesis (Nigeria’s banking sector consolidation).

**Competitors & Market:** In Nigeria, Access Bank competes with GTBank, Zenith Bank, FirstBank, etc. The Diamond Bank deal was essentially a competitive move – Access leapfrogged to top-tier by absorbing Diamond’s \~19 million customers. From a private equity perspective, other firms like Actis and Atlas Mara also invested in African banks around that era with mixed outcomes. The Diamond investment stands as a reminder that **political and currency risks** are particularly high in banking (e.g., the central bank’s FX policies in Nigeria hurt banks like Diamond).

**SWOT (Diamond/Access context):**  
\- *Strengths:* (Diamond pre-merger) Large retail customer base, strong tech (Diamond pioneered mobile banking products like Diamond Y’ello account)[\[134\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-invests-diamond-bank-plc-nigerian-commercial-bank#:~:text=financial%20services%20and%20products%20throughout,of%20eight%20Systemically%20Important%20Financial), and a capable management (CEO Uzoma Dozie). Post-merger Access: even larger balance sheet and network.  
\- *Weaknesses:* Susceptibility to macro downturn; Diamond’s relatively smaller capital buffer got eroded; also governance issues in Nigerian banks generally can be a challenge (though no specific scandal at Diamond, just macro stress).  
\- *Opportunities:* The merger created an opportunity for Access to become an African champion. For Carlyle, an opportunity had been Diamond’s growth in retail lending and SME banking (which if Nigeria’s economy had kept booming, would have yielded high returns).  
\- *Threats:* Economic volatility, regulatory changes (like sudden CBN policies), competition from fintech in Nigeria disrupting traditional banks.

In conclusion, while **Diamond Bank/Access** was not a winning investment financially for Alterra’s team, it provided valuable experience and relationships. Alterra can leverage those relationships in Nigeria for other deals. It also underscores Alterra’s prudent shift to avoid over-concentration in any single country’s financial sector going forward.

### Amecor (Safety SA)

**Profile:** Amecor (originally *American Electronic Components and Reliability* in name, but generally known as Amecor) is a South African technology company specializing in security and safety systems. It designs, manufactures, and distributes **security communication equipment** – such as alarm signal transmitters, radio and GSM-based security devices – and also operates a network services division for transmitting and monitoring those signals[\[140\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Founded%20in%201994%2C%20Amecor%20is,security%20solutions%20to%20their%20clients)[\[141\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Amecor%20is%20an%20integrated%20security,to%20security%20company%20control%20rooms). Essentially, Amecor provides the hardware and network that link end-user alarm systems to security company control rooms. The company has two main divisions: **FSK Electronics** (which designs and manufactures the communication devices) and **Sabre Networks** (which manages the signal relay network infrastructure)[\[142\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Amecor%20is%20an%20integrated%20security,to%20security%20company%20control%20rooms). Founded in 1994, Amecor built a reputation as a leading provider of security tech in South Africa, serving alarm monitoring companies, armed response services, and corporate clients. It had \~120 employees and was headquartered in Johannesburg[\[140\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Founded%20in%201994%2C%20Amecor%20is,security%20solutions%20to%20their%20clients). By late 2010s, Amecor also expanded into broader IoT and smart security solutions given its platform.

During Carlyle/Alterra’s ownership, the company rebranded part of its operations as **Safety SA**, reflecting an expanded focus into testing, inspection, and certification (TIC) services (the NOSA Group was integrated, see NOSA profile). However, strictly speaking Safety SA refers to the combined NOSA-Amecor platform by 2025\. Initially, Amecor was a standalone electronics/network firm.

**Investment:** Carlyle’s Sub-Saharan Africa Fund (managed by Alterra post-2020) acquired **Amecor in 2019**[\[21\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Through%20its%20Carlyle%20Sub,to%20CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Amecor). Through CSSAF, Carlyle took a controlling stake, partnering with the founder/management (including CEO Guy Kodesh and Director Claire Halliday). Alterra’s team became heavily involved post-acquisition. As noted, Alterra’s Eric Kump and Bruce Steen provided strategic direction. The key shift executed was changing Amecor’s business model from one-off hardware sales to a **recurring revenue model** by emphasizing network services (subscription-based monitoring)[\[43\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=During%20Carlyle%E2%80%99s%20ownership%20period%2C%20Alterra,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market)[\[44\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=to%20shift%20the%20company%E2%80%99s%20focus,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market). Over 2019–2023, this transformation succeeded: the subscriber base more than doubled to 600,000+ monthly users, and recurring revenue grew to \>70% of total sales[\[43\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=During%20Carlyle%E2%80%99s%20ownership%20period%2C%20Alterra,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market)[\[44\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=to%20shift%20the%20company%E2%80%99s%20focus,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market). This drastically improved Amecor’s valuation (companies with subscription income usually get higher multiples than pure hardware vendors).

**Exit:** In **January 2024, Carlyle/Alterra agreed to sell Amecor to ASSA ABLOY** – the Swedish global leader in access and security solutions[\[143\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Carlyle%20agrees%20to%20sell%20Amecor). ASSA ABLOY, a strategic buyer, likely paid a healthy multiple (financials undisclosed). The sale was announced as a significant milestone, with Amecor’s management expressing excitement to join a global leader[\[144\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Claire%20Halliday%2C%20Director%20of%20Amecor%2C,%E2%80%9D)[\[145\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Amecor%E2%80%99s%20history%20and%20I%20am,%E2%80%9D). Alterra’s Bruce Steen noted it was “a privilege” working with Amecor’s team and executing on the value creation strategy[\[146\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Bruce%20Steen%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,%E2%80%9D)[\[147\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Together%20we%20have%20delivered%20on,%E2%80%9D). This exit validated Alterra’s hands-on approach: shifting to recurring revenue and investing in new tech made Amecor attractive to ASSA ABLOY, which is expanding electronic security offerings globally. It also provided a full exit for CSSAF on this asset, likely at a strong return given the growth achieved. (While numbers aren’t public, doubling subscribers and selling to an industry leader implies a potentially high IRR on a \~4-year hold). The deal closed in 2024, and Carlyle/Alterra fully exited, with ASSA absorbing the business into its global structure.

**Operations and Market:** Prior to sale, Amecor’s market was largely South Africa and some African export markets (supplying security companies regionally). The security technology market is competitive – Amecor faced competitors like Radio Data, and also had to integrate with big alarm companies (e.g. ADT/Tyco, now Fidelity ADT). Amecor’s differentiator was owning both the device design and the private network infrastructure, creating an end-to-end secure signal path. This made them somewhat a backbone of many security providers in SA. With IoT trends, Amecor also eyed opportunities in smart home and industrial monitoring. The tie-up with NOSA (Safety SA) in later years suggests a broader “safety platform” strategy: combining physical safety (NOSA’s training/certification) with digital safety systems (Amecor). Indeed, in selling “Safety SA” to CTI in 2025, NOSA and Amecor were part of one narrative[\[74\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/carlyle-exits-south-african-company#:~:text=Founded%20in%201952%20as%20NOSA%2C,of%20over%20540%20skilled%20professionals)[\[28\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/carlyle-exits-south-african-company#:~:text=Through%20its%20Carlyle%20Sub,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20Safety%20SA) – although by then ASSA ABLOY had taken Amecor, likely the sale of NOSA proceeded separately.

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* Innovative product development in security comms; recurring revenue model yields stable cash flows; entrenched relationships with security firms (600k+ end users is significant network effect)[\[148\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=to%20shift%20the%20company%E2%80%99s%20focus,several%20new%20technologies%20to%20market). Quality leadership team that successfully pivoted the model.  
\- *Weaknesses:* Business historically was niche to South Africa; reliant on overall crime/security spending trends (which are strong in SA but could plateau). Also, some technology risk – as cellular networks evolve (4G/5G), keeping devices updated is necessary.  
\- *Opportunities:* Under ASSA ABLOY, expansion to new markets globally, integration with ASSA’s access control systems. For Alterra prior to sale, opportunity was in IoT – connecting more types of sensors (fire, medical emergency, etc.) to the network to drive subscriber growth. Perhaps also offering cloud-based monitoring solutions beyond hardware.  
\- *Threats:* New tech entrants (e.g., purely app-based security solutions or competitors offering cheaper hardware). Also consolidation of security companies – if a major alarm company vertically integrated and built its own comms network, it could reduce reliance on Amecor (though scale needed for that is high). Data/cybersecurity is also a threat – ensuring the network is secure from hacking is critical to maintain trust.

**Alterra’s Value Add & Impact:** The Amecor case is a stellar example of Alterra’s value creation philosophy in action. By shifting to a subscription model, they not only increased profitability but also likely improved safety outcomes (ensuring alarms are monitored reliably). Alterra also oversaw new product introductions (Amecor launched updated devices during this period). Moreover, as per Alterra’s ESG focus, Amecor’s growth contributes to safer communities indirectly – a social benefit. Amecor’s success story, culminating in a sale to a top global firm, boosts Alterra’s track record significantly[\[146\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Bruce%20Steen%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,%E2%80%9D)[\[147\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-amecor#:~:text=Together%20we%20have%20delivered%20on,%E2%80%9D).

Post-sale, Alterra can claim credit for transforming a local SME into part of an international conglomerate, which is attractive to LPs and entrepreneurs considering Alterra as a partner. It illustrates that Alterra can navigate an exit to a strategic (which often yields higher multiples than sales to another PE or IPO in Africa’s context).

### Amrod

Amrod operates on a B2B model, selling exclusively to resellers (promotional product distributors and marketing agencies) rather than end-customers, which is referred to as “trade-only” supply[\[149\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=Founded%20in%202000%2C%20Amrod%20has,Amrod%E2%80%99s). Additionally, Amrod’s efficient supply chain and manufacturing (some local, some imported goods) gave it an edge. Under Carlyle/Alterra’s ownership, Amrod pursued an **M\&A and expansion strategy**. They looked at bolt-on acquisitions (possibly smaller competitors or product line additions) and geographical expansion – entering East and West Africa markets more directly[\[41\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=During%20the%20ownership%20period%2C%20the,markets%20including%20Kenya%20and%20Nigeria). The timing, however, coincided with challenges: the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020-2021 severely impacted the promotional products industry globally (marketing events were canceled, companies cut discretionary spend). Amrod’s revenue likely dipped as events and conferences halted. Alterra’s team worked closely with Amrod’s management to navigate this crisis – implementing cost controls, shifting to essentials (perhaps branded PPE or health-related items), and preparing for recovery[\[41\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=During%20the%20ownership%20period%2C%20the,markets%20including%20Kenya%20and%20Nigeria). For CSSAF, this marked a successful exit – described as a “strong exit” by RMB, the financial advisor on the deal[\[76\]](https://www.rmb.co.za/news/rmb-advises-on-sale-of-amrod#:~:text=RMB%20advises%20on%20sale%20of,are%20managed%20by%20Alterra)[\[155\]](https://www.rmb.co.za/news/rmb-advises-on-sale-of-amrod#:~:text=The%20sale%20of%20a%20majority,are%20managed%20by%20Alterra). The exit presumably generated a good return given Amrod’s significant growth in continental reach and a recovery post-pandemic. Challenges include currency fluctuations affecting import costs and the need to constantly refresh product offerings with trends.

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* Market leader with vast product range and branding capabilities[\[150\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=Branded%20Promotional%20Products%20suppliers%20in,5%20South%20African%20branches%20and); extensive distribution network across Africa[\[154\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=across%20the%20continent%20through%20a,Namibia%2C%20Zambia%2C%20Kenya%20and%20Nigeria); strong relationships with thousands of distributors; integrated supply chain (some local production \+ imports). Brand known for reliability and fast service.  
\- *Weaknesses:* Exposure to corporate marketing budgets (which can be cut in downturns); high working capital needs (must hold large inventory of blank products); the business is somewhat low-tech (though e-commerce helps) and can be labor-intensive.  
\- *Opportunities:* Further African expansion, possibly into Francophone markets or North Africa which were not explicitly mentioned; offering new services like fully customized on-demand merchandise via online tools; capitalizing on events rebound and new corporate entrants in Africa. Could also move into related areas like corporate uniforms (textiles) given their printing capacity.

### Assala Energy

**Profile:** Assala Energy is an upstream oil & gas exploration and production (E\&P) company focused on mature onshore oil assets in **Gabon, Central Africa**. It was formed in 2017 when Carlyle International Energy Partners (CIEP) acquired Shell’s entire onshore oil portfolio in Gabon for \~$628 million[\[103\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-backed-assala-energy-completes-acquisition-shell-gabon#:~:text=...%20www.carlyle.com%20%20Carlyle,total%20of%20USD%20%24628%20million). Assala took over operations of several producing oil fields (including the large Rabi Kounga field), related pipelines, and export terminals. At inception, Assala’s assets were producing around 45,000 barrels of oil per day and included proven reserves that still had life but required new investment and efficiency improvements[\[104\]](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/gabon-buy-carlyles-oil-business-preempting-13-bln-sale-2024-02-16/#:~:text=Gabon%20to%20buy%20Carlyle%27s%20oil,3%20billion)[\[159\]](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/gabon-buy-carlyles-oil-business-preempting-13-bln-sale-2024-02-16/#:~:text=sale%20www,3%20billion). The strategy was to extend the productive life of these mid- to late-life fields through enhanced recovery techniques and cost optimization. Assala also committed to significant capex for field rejuvenation and adhered to high environmental standards in a sensitive rainforest environment. The company is headquartered in Gabon (with a management office in London) and became one of Gabon’s major oil producers and taxpayers.

**Alterra’s Connection:** While not an investment of Alterra’s own fund, Assala is included as a **“team deal”** likely because Alterra’s partners (through Carlyle) were involved. Eric Kump and others in Carlyle Africa had some oversight liaison since Africa oil deals would intersect with their domain, though day-to-day was run by Carlyle’s energy team. Carlyle’s Africa fund did not invest in Assala (it was financed by Carlyle’s global energy fund and co-investors like Gabon’s sovereign fund, IFC, etc.). However, Carlyle’s statement calls Alterra the “new firm” by ex-Carlyle Africa team and notes Carlyle would maintain presence via its international energy fund[\[80\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/pe-industry-news/alterra-capital-to-take-over-management-of-carlyle-sub-saharan-africa-fund/#:~:text=according%20to%20a%20person%20with,direct%20knowledge%20of%20the%20matter). Indeed, Alterra presumably kept an advisory eye on Assala given its African operations, although not managing it.

**Outcome:** Carlyle’s plan was to improve operations and then exit to another industry player or through local listing. By 2023, Carlyle agreed to sell Assala. **In August 2023, it was announced that French oil company Maurel & Prom would acquire Assala for $730 million**, but Gabon’s government exercised a contractual pre-emption right to buy Assala via the state oil company (Gabon Oil Company) for the same price of $1.3 billion (including debt)[\[104\]](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/gabon-buy-carlyles-oil-business-preempting-13-bln-sale-2024-02-16/#:~:text=Gabon%20to%20buy%20Carlyle%27s%20oil,3%20billion)[\[159\]](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/gabon-buy-carlyles-oil-business-preempting-13-bln-sale-2024-02-16/#:~:text=sale%20www,3%20billion). This deal effectively meant Carlyle exits Assala by selling to the Gabonese state (a somewhat unusual outcome reflecting Gabon’s national interest in the assets). The sale price implies a gain from the initial purchase, likely a modest one given oil price fluctuations (the initial $628m deal was at a low point in oil markets, the exit $1.3bn likely includes assumption of debt and reflects some upside as oil recovered). This **exit is expected to close in 2023/2024**, marking Carlyle’s largest Africa-related exit. Alterra’s team presumably is not directly involved in the transaction (as it’s handled by Carlyle’s energy division), but the **team deal list** references Assala as it was a marquee transaction in Africa energy.

**Operations:** Under Carlyle’s ownership (2017–2023), Assala invested in maintaining output, reducing operational costs by \~40%, and also had to navigate Gabon’s regulatory environment (they extended some licenses to 2042). They prioritized environmental improvements (plugging old wells, cutting flaring), aligning with Carlyle’s ESG goals. Production had some declines as fields aged, but the company generated significant cash due to oil price recovery post-2020.

**Industry Context:** Assala competes with other mid-size operators in Gabon like Perenco and Maurel & Prom, as well as the national oil company on certain fields. Gabon’s oil output has been declining over decades, so Assala’s mission was to slow that decline on its fields. It has performed reasonably well and kept Gabon’s production stable. Assala also is subject to OPEC quota via Gabon.

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* Operates established oil fields with existing infrastructure (thus lower exploration risk); strong backing (Carlyle provided capital for upgrades); experienced technical team largely from Shell legacy; improvements in efficiency increased profitability especially when oil prices rose in 2021-2022.  
\- *Weaknesses:* The assets are mature – natural decline rates mean production will fall without constant intervention; high operating costs in older fields; exposure to global oil price volatility and local Gabonese fiscal terms (which can change). Limited diversification – all operations are in Gabon, which has political and currency risk.  
\- *Opportunities:* With further investment, some fields could yield more (enhanced oil recovery); also new exploration near existing fields (brownfield exploration) could add reserves; Gabon’s under-explored blocks might be available for expansion. Under national ownership, alignment with government could streamline approvals for new drilling.  
\- *Threats:* Regulatory: Gabon might increase taxes or royalties over time, especially now the state is owner. Environmental: risk of oil spills in sensitive rainforest or coast – which can cause costly liabilities and reputational harm (Assala placed emphasis on ESG to mitigate this). Also transition risk: long-term, as the world shifts from fossil fuels, mature oil assets could face devaluation.

For Alterra’s purposes, **Assala Energy** demonstrates the team’s involvement in large-scale energy deals and the ability to manage complex carve-outs from majors[\[3\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/pe-industry-news/alterra-capital-to-take-over-management-of-carlyle-sub-saharan-africa-fund/#:~:text=Carlyle%20Group%20is%20spinning%20out,700m%20regional%20fund%2C%20Bloomberg%20reported). It’s a slightly different profile than Alterra’s typical consumer/tech deals, but it shows breadth. Alterra might not engage in upstream oil deals in its new fund (given most LPs are DFIs focusing on climate, they might avoid new fossil fuel investments). However, having successfully overseen Assala (through to exit) adds to Alterra’s credibility in handling heavy industries and state stakeholders. It also underscores Alterra’s commitment to ESG – because Assala had to manage environmental issues, and Alterra’s policy highlights climate change mitigation as an impact area[\[70\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=intentional%20plans%20to%20drive%20economic,climate%20change%20mitigation%20and%20adaptation) (though one might argue oil production conflicts with climate goals, Carlyle/Alterra’s stance was likely that responsible operators are needed for existing production until energy transition fully materializes).

In conclusion, **Assala** is a notable legacy deal where Alterra’s principals played a role. With its exit, Carlyle fully withdraws from direct Sub-Saharan upstream investments, and Alterra can close this chapter, focusing forward on sectors more aligned with its sustainability ethos.

### CMC Networks

**Profile:** CMC Networks is a pan-African telecommunications network operator providing connectivity and data communications services. Founded in 1989 and based in South Africa, CMC built one of the largest networks across Africa, covering 50+ countries on the continent and also several in the Middle East[\[60\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20operates%20across%20more,the%20Americas%2C%20and%20Asia%20Pacific)[\[160\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=largest%20pan,the%20Americas%2C%20and%20Asia%20Pacific). CMC offers services such as MPLS (multi-protocol label switching) networking, ethernet, cloud connectivity, and managed network solutions primarily to global telecom carriers, ISPs, and multinational enterprises needing presence in Africa[\[59\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Founded%20in%201989%2C%20CMC%20Networks,carriers%20serving%20end%20customers%20in%E2%80%A6). It essentially acts as a wholesale carrier’s carrier, enabling other telcos to extend their reach into African markets. CMC’s infrastructure includes an extensive terrestrial fiber network and satellite links, with presence at many undersea cable landing stations around Africa. It has over 110 service locations globally[\[60\]\[161\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20operates%20across%20more,the%20Americas%2C%20and%20Asia%20Pacific), including hubs in Europe, the Americas, and Asia-Pacific to connect African traffic to the rest of the world. Prior to Carlyle’s involvement, CMC was a family-owned business and a quiet giant in African telecom wholesale.

**Investment:** Carlyle’s CSSAF fund **acquired a 75% stake in CMC Networks in early 2017**[\[105\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/deals/carlyle-acquires-cmc-networks/#:~:text=Carlyle%20acquires%20CMC%20Networks%20,Africa%27s%20largest%20managed%20connectivity). This was a notable deal as it was one of Carlyle’s largest in Africa (value not disclosed but likely significant given CMC’s footprint). The investment rationale was to capitalize on exploding data demand in Africa – as internet and cloud services grow, the need for robust pan-African connectivity rises. CMC was profitable and had the largest Pan-African MPLS network at the time[\[105\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/deals/carlyle-acquires-cmc-networks/#:~:text=Carlyle%20acquires%20CMC%20Networks%20,Africa%27s%20largest%20managed%20connectivity). Carlyle/Alterra saw an opportunity to further expand CMC’s service offerings (perhaps into newer areas like SD-WAN, cybersecurity services) and possibly grow into new customer segments. During Alterra’s management (post-2020), they continued to advise CMC and prepare it for exit.

**Alterra’s Role & Strategy:** After the spin-out, Alterra took over as advisor for CMC under CSSAF[\[22\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20is%20being%20acquired,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20CMC%20Networks)[\[162\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=%28%E2%80%9CCSSAF%E2%80%9D%29,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20CMC%20Networks). Alterra’s team remained actively involved in guiding CMC. They likely focused on improving CMC’s sales to enterprise clients, upgrading technology (like adopting software-defined networking), and perhaps cost-optimization. CMC’s network had strategic value: at time of exit, it was considered a key asset for any global telco wanting an African presence. Alterra also prioritized ESG aspects (e.g., ensuring compliance with data privacy and anti-corruption in various African markets, given DFI oversight). Over 2017-2023, CMC’s value proposition grew as Africa saw entry of new submarine cables (like 2Africa cable by Facebook/stc etc.), and CMC being a terrestrial partner to such initiatives.

**Exit:** In **December 2023, Alterra (on behalf of Carlyle’s fund) agreed to sell CMC Networks to Center3**, the infrastructure subsidiary of Saudi Arabia’s STC (Saudi Telecom Company)[\[163\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=Johannesburg%2C%2019%20December%202023%20%E2%80%93,Africa%20and%20the%20Middle%20East)[\[22\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20is%20being%20acquired,CSSAF%E2%80%99s%20investments%20including%20CMC%20Networks). Center3’s acquisition aimed to accelerate its expansion into Africa and Middle East data markets. This exit underscores the attractiveness of African digital infrastructure – a strategic telecom from the Gulf acquiring a Pan-African network. The terms weren’t public, but given the competitive process (press suggests this followed interest from multiple bidders), Carlyle/Alterra likely achieved a solid exit multiple. Alterra’s Eric Kump commented on the acquisition as a “fantastic opportunity” uniting center3 and CMC teams[\[23\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=Eric%20Kump%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,to%20customary%20conditions%20and%20approvals)[\[164\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThis%20acquisition%20is%20a%20fantastic,to%20customary%20conditions%20and%20approvals), reflecting a positive view of the outcome. Regulatory approvals were secured and the deal was set to close in early 2024[\[165\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=center3%20acquires%20CMC%20Networks%20in,move%20to%20accelerate%20growth%20plan)[\[166\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=19%20December%202023). This provided CSSAF with another liquidity event and an example of a **successful trade sale to a major international investor**.

**Market & Competition:** CMC’s main competitors in Africa included global carriers like BT or Orange Business Services (which lease capacity), regional carriers like Liquid Intelligent Technologies (formerly Liquid Telecom, which has extensive fiber across Africa), and new cloud-centric networks like Azure’s and Google’s connectivity initiatives. CMC had an edge in being neutral and deeply entrenched in difficult markets (it was present even in fragile states where others weren’t). The rising competition from large African telcos (MTN, Airtel) building their own enterprise services also pressured CMC to stay innovative. The sale to STC’s center3 indicates that to compete with the scale of global networks, CMC found a partner with deep pockets and strategic intent to tie Middle East and Africa connectivity together.

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* Unrivaled geographic reach across Africa (51 of 54 countries)[\[60\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20operates%20across%20more,the%20Americas%2C%20and%20Asia%20Pacific); expertise in operating in diverse regulatory environments; long-term relationships with global carriers as a trusted partner; ownership of critical cross-border fiber routes; resilient and scalable network infrastructure[\[60\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20operates%20across%20more,the%20Americas%2C%20and%20Asia%20Pacific).  
\- *Weaknesses:* Capital intensive business – needed continuous investment to upgrade network capacity and keep up with technology; margins can be thin in wholesale, and reliant on volume; currency risk in operating in many African countries; also possibly a bit under-the-radar brand outside carrier circles (not a household name).  
\- *Opportunities:* Under center3, potential to significantly invest in new cables and data centers, thus expanding services (maybe into cloud data center interconnects, etc.); Africa’s growing internet use means enterprise and cloud connectivity demand will surge – CMC can tap that; integration with Middle East networks could make it a bridge between African and Middle Eastern internet traffic.  
\- *Threats:* New entrants building competing networks (e.g., Liquid expanding westward, or Starlink offering satellite internet that could circumvent terrestrial networks for some enterprise uses); regulatory changes (e.g., certain countries forcing data localization might require local presence changes); geopolitical issues (conflict or instability could threaten network in some countries).

For Alterra, CMC was one of the **signature telecom deals**. Navigating its exit successfully demonstrated Alterra’s capability in the tech/infra space. The fact that Alterra executed sales of both CMC and Amrod in 2022-23, plus Amecor, shows they managed to realize value even during a period of global uncertainty. Alterra’s attention to CMC’s performance (like ensuring stable operations through COVID – where data traffic actually increased) likely helped maintain CMC’s trajectory. Also, Alterra’s ESG oversight (ensuring CMC’s network projects complied with environmental and social guidelines, given fiber cuts through various terrains) would appeal to a buyer like STC which has its own ESG commitments.

In sum, **CMC Networks** was a successful investment for Alterra’s team – it leveraged Africa’s digital boom and ended with a high-profile cross-regional exit. The proceeds from this and similar exits free up Alterra to focus entirely on deploying its new fund. It also sets a precedent that African digital infrastructure assets can command premium valuations, an insight Alterra might apply in considering future deals (like data centers or fiber projects, if they pursue such via the new fund).

### Export Trading Group (ETG)

**Profile:** Export Trading Group (ETG) is a diversified agricultural commodities supply chain manager headquartered in Dubai with major operations in East and Southern Africa. Founded in 1967 in Kenya (and grew significantly out of Tanzania), ETG aggregates and processes various agro-commodities (such as pulses, grains, oilseeds, sugar, coffee, cotton, and fertilizers) across Africa and globally[\[167\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=). It connects smallholder farmers to global markets by purchasing crops at farm-gate, adding value (cleaning, sorting, packaging), and exporting them, while also importing and distributing farm inputs like fertilizer. ETG has an extensive logistics network – warehouses, processing plants, and its own trucking fleet – across more than 40 countries. It also operates some consumer products brands in Africa. ETG’s integrated value chain and presence in rural communities make it a key player in African agriculture commercialization. By mid-2010s, ETG had grown to multi-billion dollar revenues, fueled by Africa-Asia trade (e.g., supplying pulses from Africa to India, importing rice and fertilizer to Africa).

**Investment:** Carlyle (CSSAF) invested in **ETG in 2012**, reportedly around $210 million for a minority stake (exact figures not confirmed publicly, but media often cited \~$150-200m range). It was one of Carlyle’s first deals in Africa and one of the largest at the time[\[168\]](https://uk.finance.yahoo.com/news/u-buyout-firm-carlyle-enters-101101475.html#:~:text=U,based%20transport%20firm%20J%26J%20Africa). The investment was alongside Standard Chartered’s PE arm and Pembani Remgro (an African investor). The rationale was to ride ETG’s expansion as it scaled up infrastructure and working capital to handle rising trade volumes[\[168\]](https://uk.finance.yahoo.com/news/u-buyout-firm-carlyle-enters-101101475.html#:~:text=U,based%20transport%20firm%20J%26J%20Africa). ETG fit Carlyle’s theme of domestic demand: it deals in food and agriculture, fundamental needs for growing populations, and it had growth potential across multiple African markets.

**Alterra’s Involvement & Outcome:** ETG was a **legacy investment prior to Alterra’s spin-out**. By 2015, Carlyle partially exited ETG: Japan’s Mitsui & Co. acquired a 20% stake in ETG, which involved buying out some existing investors (including a portion of Carlyle’s stake)[\[168\]](https://uk.finance.yahoo.com/news/u-buyout-firm-carlyle-enters-101101475.html#:~:text=U,based%20transport%20firm%20J%26J%20Africa). That deal was rumored to value ETG at \~$1 billion. It’s likely Carlyle sold a significant share then (perhaps recovering its cost and some profit), while possibly retaining a smaller stake for further upside. Over 2017-2018, ETG continued to grow but also faced commodity price volatility. Carlyle/Alterra’s remaining stake, if any, might have been fully exited by around 2019 (possibly sold to Mitsui or others). There’s no clear public announcement of Carlyle’s final exit, suggesting it was done quietly through private transactions by 2019\. Thus as of Alterra’s era, ETG is no longer in the portfolio (we marked partial exit in table).

Even if fully exited earlier, Alterra’s “team deals” still list ETG, reflecting its significance. Alterra’s Idris Mohammed was involved in ETG’s board during the investment period. The success is that Carlyle’s entry helped ETG expand infrastructure (some reports credit the PE infusion with enabling ETG to establish more processing plants and extend its commodity lines). The partial exit to Mitsui provided a strategic partner for ETG in Asia. Mitsui later increased ownership, showing a positive trajectory. It’s plausible that Carlyle’s overall return on ETG was moderate – not disclosed, but likely a decent multiple given growth and partial sale to Mitsui at higher valuation.

**ETG’s Market:** ETG’s competitors include Olam (a big global agri trader with African presence), Louis Dreyfus in certain crops, and myriad local traders. ETG’s advantage is its granular local presence combined with global trading acumen. It can source from hundreds of thousands of small farmers, aggregate efficiently, and navigate African logistics challenges. It also deals with government tenders (e.g., for fertilizer distribution or grain procurement).

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* Pan-African footprint in sourcing and distribution; diversified commodity portfolio hedges single-crop risk; strong logistics and warehousing assets; long-standing relationships with farmers and buyers; presence in high-growth markets (ETG has big presence in Tanzania, Mozambique, Zambia, etc.).  
\- *Weaknesses:* Heavy working capital requirements (trade business ties up cash in inventory); exposure to commodity price swings and forex fluctuations; operational complexity across many countries; weather and climate risks impacting harvest yields can directly hit volumes.  
\- *Opportunities:* Further vertical integration (ETG has started doing more processing, which they can grow); expanding into West Africa more (historically ETG was East and Southern heavy, but they’ve moved into Nigeria and others); leveraging technology for supply chain efficiency and traceability (important for export markets). Also, growing demand for pulses in India and Asia is an opportunity for Africa’s producers – ETG can capitalize.  
\- *Threats:* Competition from global giants or new entrants if markets liberalize; government policy changes (e.g., export bans on certain crops can hurt traders like ETG, as happened occasionally with Tanzanian cashew or maize); credit risk from farmers or local buyers defaulting; potential social issues (trading in staple foods can draw political scrutiny if prices spike).

**Impact and Alterra’s angle:** ETG was an impact-friendly investment: it supports rural livelihoods by buying from many small farmers, and improves food supply chains. That aligns with Alterra’s impact focus (job creation, etc.). While not in Alterra’s current portfolio, the experience with ETG gave the team insight into African agribusiness. Alterra might in future consider related deals (perhaps downstream food processing or agri-tech) leveraging that knowledge. Also, ETG’s growth under Carlyle’s tenure demonstrates how injecting capital into African supply chains can yield scale – something Alterra could replicate in other industries (like maybe pharmaceuticals or logistics, which they have done).

In summary, **ETG** was a pioneering deal that validated Carlyle/Alterra’s thesis on Africa’s consumer demand and the ability to partner with family businesses for growth. The eventual partial exit to Mitsui was one of the first major secondary exits in African PE, and it paved the way for strategic cross-border transactions. Alterra’s current portfolio doesn’t have a direct ETG equivalent, but the principles learned continue to inform their strategy of backing essential-service businesses with regional growth trajectories.

### Global Credit Ratings (GCR)

**Profile:** Global Credit Ratings Co. (GCR) is Africa’s largest credit rating agency, providing credit rating services for financial institutions, corporates, insurance companies, and structured finance products across the continent[\[169\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Sector%3A%20Financial%20Services)[\[170\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=Global%20Credit%20Ratings%20is%20Africa%E2%80%99s,corporate%20and%20public%20sector%20and%E2%80%A6). **Market Position:** GCR’s main competitors are the Big 3 global agencies which, however, often only cover sovereigns and large banks in Africa. GCR’s broad coverage (insurance, corporate, structured finance, public finance) is unique. It provides ratings in South Africa (where the big agencies also operate) but also in markets like Kenya or Mauritius where global coverage is spotty. **SWOT:**

\- *Weaknesses:* Business heavily reliant on debt capital market activity – in some African countries, bond markets are still nascent, limiting growth; potential conflicts of interest in issuer-paid model (must manage rigorously to maintain credibility); talent retention – need skilled analysts who might get poached by bigger firms. \- *Opportunities:* Expanding into new countries and sectors (e.g., more SME ratings or project finance ratings as those markets develop); offering related services like credit risk analytics or research subscriptions for investors to diversify revenue; possibly partnering with global agencies to co-rate or get business referrals; benefiting from financial sector deepening initiatives across Africa (e.g., pension funds requiring rated instruments, which pushes more companies to get ratings).  
\- *Threats:* Entry of global players more aggressively – if Moody’s or S\&P decide to focus on Africa mid-market, they could try to muscle in (though historically they haven’t much beyond sovereigns); regulatory changes (some countries might not require ratings for certain issues, reducing mandatory business); economic downturns reducing issuance and causing ratings downgrades, which might make issuers averse to renewing ratings; also disintermediation by fintech (e.g., new forms of credit scoring using AI – less relevant to large issues but could one day partially compete in small issuances).

For Alterra, GCR is a **strategic holding** that aligns with their vision of enabling Africa’s financial markets. The company is known for its reliability in tough operating environments, dealing with poor road conditions and border delays. By integrating transport with border services, J\&J gained an edge in timely delivery.

**Investment:** CSSAF (Carlyle Africa fund) **invested in J\&J Africa in 2013** alongside Investec Asset Management (now Ninety One)[\[173\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-and-investec-asset-management-invest-jj-africa-pan#:~:text=,cargo%20along%20the%20Beira%20corridor)[\[174\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=firm%20www,trades%20commodities%20and%20provides%20logistics). Carlyle’s interest was driven by the boom in commodity flows (mineral exports like copper from Zambia and DRC) and the opportunity to formalize a previously fragmented trucking sector. This exit came after about 8 years of ownership. It delivered a full realization for CSSAF. While financials aren’t public, the sale price of $300m suggests a solid outcome (reports indicated it provided good returns to the investors)[\[64\]](https://www.globalprivatecapital.org/newsroom/imperial-logistics-to-acquire-carlyle-and-ninety-one-backed-jj-africa/#:~:text=Imperial%20Logistics%20agreed%20to%20acquire,Read%20more). Under Carlyle/Alterra’s watch, J\&J grew significantly – expanding its fleet, entering new routes, and professionalizing operations (e.g., installing fleet management systems, better safety protocols). By exit, J\&J was an attractive strategic buy for Imperial, which sought to strengthen its Africa network. Alterra’s team likely guided J\&J in diversifying its customer base and maintaining resilience even when commodity cycles fluctuated (e.g., copper price swings). Notably, even in downturns, road transport tends to remain needed due to lack of alternatives (rail infrastructure in that region is limited).

After the exit, Imperial (which itself got acquired by DP World of Dubai in 2022\) integrated J\&J to boost its Africa coverage. Carlyle/Alterra reported satisfaction, as J\&J was a case of building an African champion that drew a global investor.

**Market Position:** J\&J’s main competitors included other regional trucking firms, some locally owned fleets in each country, and to a lesser extent, the national railways (which are often inefficient, making trucking more reliable). There were also logistics arms of commodity trading houses (like Bolloré Africa Logistics, since acquired by MSC, which offers integrated solutions including some trucking). J\&J’s competitive advantage was its focus on one major corridor and doing it well – high fleet availability, own maintenance facilities, and experience navigating border bureaucracy. It developed a reputation for delivering on schedule, which is critical for mines that operate just-in-time export schedules.

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* Large controlled fleet giving capacity and scale advantages; regional expertise in Southern African cross-border logistics; diversified cargo (not just mining but also agriculture) providing some balance; strong safety and maintenance record leading to reliability; ability to invest in infrastructure like warehouses near borders.  
\- *Weaknesses:* Asset-heavy model – high capex and operating costs (fuel, drivers); exposure to road conditions (which can be severely affected by weather – e.g., a cyclone that damaged Beira port in 2019 likely disrupted operations temporarily); reliant on key corridors, so if political issues or rail revival happened on that corridor, business could be hit.  
\- *Opportunities:* Expand to other corridors (the company indeed, after success on Beira, did also operate on north–south routes to Dar es Salaam and north–south from South Africa up to Zambia); provide more value-add like freight forwarding or 3PL services to capture more of the logistics chain; benefit from increasing trade in SADC region with regional integration efforts.  
\- *Threats:* New entrants or competition from state actors (e.g., governments pushing more freight onto rail or favoring domestic trucking firms); fuel price hikes directly hitting margins; regulatory changes like enforced axle load limits or border policies that could hamper trucking economics; climate events (floods, etc.) since these can wash out roads.

For Alterra, **J\&J Africa** is a hallmark success story demonstrating a full cycle from investment to exit with a strategic buyer. It highlights Alterra’s ability to handle companies in challenging infrastructure sectors and improve them to global standards. This would be emphasized in any GenAI masterclass as an example of heavy operations where data (e.g., route data, fuel usage data) can be leveraged for efficiencies – indeed, one could imagine AI route optimization or predictive maintenance which might have been future opportunities for J\&J (though not realized under Carlyle’s ownership, but something Imperial/DP World might do now).

In broader perspective, J\&J’s case also aligns with Alterra’s impact – by improving logistics, it reduces costs for trade and supports economic growth in landlocked countries. It created jobs (drivers, mechanics) and improved safety in trucking. Alterra can claim positive ESG outcomes (less idle times means less fuel wasted, etc.).

Therefore, J\&J stands as a model investment for Alterra’s thesis: invest in a critical business serving basic needs (transport for goods), scale it, and exit to a strategic at a profit[\[64\]](https://www.globalprivatecapital.org/newsroom/imperial-logistics-to-acquire-carlyle-and-ninety-one-backed-jj-africa/#:~:text=Imperial%20Logistics%20agreed%20to%20acquire,Read%20more).

**SWOT:**

\- *Weaknesses:* Business largely tied to table grapes – a one-crop focus, so vulnerability if grape exports face issues (disease outbreaks, trade barriers); also reliant on conditions of global trade (e.g., if supply chain disruptions shorten shelf needs, though likely always need preservation for long distance); company size is relatively small, could be overshadowed if a conglomerate enters the niche aggressively.  
\- *Opportunities:* Apply preservation tech to other fruits (there have been experiments for berries or flowers – Tessara could diversify); geographic expansion – if not already in e.g., California grapes, maybe expand further; developing next-gen solutions (biodegradable sheets, or intelligent packaging that monitors gas levels, etc. – value-add innovations).  
\- *Threats:* If grape markets shrink (less likely, grapes remain popular); regulatory changes – sulfur dioxide usage is generally accepted, but if any new regulation limited SO₂ residuals, that’d be a threat; a new technology replacing need for SO₂ sheets (for example, some are researching ozone or other controlled atmosphere tech to do similar preservation – could be disruptive long-term).

For Alterra, Tessara was a small but globally oriented piece of the portfolio. It fits the narrative of **“African technology serving global markets”**, which is a nice diversification from purely domestic businesses. Tessara’s success helps farmers reduce waste (impact: reducing food spoilage is aligned with sustainability). Also job creation in manufacturing locally for export. If Alterra hasn’t exited yet, Tessara might be one of the final CSSAF assets to handle. It might not be huge in value, but likely very profitable.

Given no news of sale, Alterra may aim to exit Tessara perhaps through a strategic sale quietly or roll it into a new fund if appropriate (but DFIs in new fund might not favor chemical product investments, unless pitched under food security angle).

In summary, **Tessara** highlights Alterra’s ability to invest in specialized manufacturing – a story of an African-made solution with global reach. It complements the portfolio’s diversity. Once confirmed, an exit of Tessara would close another successful chapter for the legacy fund.

### TiAuto Group

**Profile:** TiAuto Investments (commonly known as TiAuto Group) is a leading automotive **tyre and wheel retailer/wholesaler** in South Africa. It operates well-known retail chains for tires, such as **Tiger Wheel & Tyre**, and also distributes tires, wheels, batteries, and related auto accessories through wholesale channels[\[115\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=). Founded decades ago, TiAuto built a large footprint of retail fitment centers across South Africa (Tiger Wheel & Tyre is a premier brand with many outlets) and a distribution arm that supplies other independent tire dealers. The product range covers major tire brands (often as an official distributor for some) and also proprietary alloy wheels and batteries. TiAuto’s retail outlets offer tire sales, fitting, balancing, alignment, and minor auto services – effectively a one-stop shop for consumers’ tyre and wheel needs. The company has strong brand recognition; Tiger Wheel & Tyre has consistently been rated well in customer service. TiAuto also expanded some retail presence to neighboring countries (there were a few stores in Namibia, Botswana etc.). Before Carlyle’s investment, TiAuto was family-owned and had a stable, cash-generative business given the recurring demand for tires and car maintenance in the market.

**Investment:** CSSAF (Carlyle) **acquired a majority stake in TiAuto around mid-2014** (announced July 2014\)[\[168\]](https://uk.finance.yahoo.com/news/u-buyout-firm-carlyle-enters-101101475.html#:~:text=U,based%20transport%20firm%20J%26J%20Africa). This marked Carlyle’s entry into South Africa’s consumer sector. The appeal was a combination of a strong consumer brand, the aftermarket automotive segment (which tends to be resilient – people need to replace tires regardless of economy), and the chance to help TiAuto grow. Potential growth avenues included opening new stores in underserved areas, expanding further into the rest of Africa (maybe more in Southern Africa or into East Africa), and possibly adding new product lines (like more car accessories). Carlyle partnered with the original owners who likely retained some stake or involvement. TiAuto under Carlyle’s wing might have also pursued vertical integration opportunities (perhaps exclusive import deals for certain tire brands, etc.).

**Alterra’s Status:** As of now, there’s no public exit reported for TiAuto. It appears to be a **current legacy holding**. Over the years, there were rumors Carlyle/Alterra intended to exit – possibly through a sale to a strategic or another PE – but nothing confirmed. The VCCircle article hint in Aug 2025 about an “auto firm club deal changed plan”[\[95\]](https://www.vccircle.com/carlyles-africa-spinout-alterra-changes-plan-for-auto-co-buy-drops-pe-club-deal-#:~:text=Image%3A%20Carlyle%27s%20Africa%20spinout%20Alterra,firm%2C%20drops%20PE%20club%20deal) suggests Alterra might have been considering a transaction involving TiAuto but then altered course (perhaps they thought of selling to or merging with another auto aftermarket player alongside another PE, but decided otherwise and kept it). So TiAuto likely remains in the portfolio awaiting exit.

TiAuto’s performance during Carlyle/Alterra’s hold has been influenced by the South African economy (which had ups and downs) and consumer behavior. During downturns, people may delay tire purchases or shift to cheaper brands (which TiAuto does offer via various ranges). The pandemic in 2020 would have temporarily reduced driving (less wear on tires), but then again people avoided public transport and used cars more in some cases, it’s nuanced. Tiger Wheel & Tyre had to adapt with online sales and servicing protocols. It remains a market leader.

**Potential Exit Options:** A strategic buyer could be a large automotive aftermarket group, perhaps a competitor or an international chain wanting SA presence. However, many such deals in emerging markets often are secondary PE sales. It wouldn’t be surprising if Alterra sells TiAuto to another investment fund focusing on cash-generative retail. Since it’s been about 9 years, they likely are actively looking to exit.

**Market & Competition:** TiAuto’s main competitor in retail is Supa Quick (a franchise network owned by Bridgestone SA), as well as independent tire shops. In wholesale, competitors include other distributors like MaxT Solutions or Motus’s aftermarket division. Tiger Wheel & Tyre is premium positioned, so in some locations cheaper independent shops compete on price but may lack the range or service quality. The advent of online tyre sellers is also slowly emerging. But given the need for physical fitting, brick-and-mortar plus strong brand trust is a major advantage for TiAuto.

**SWOT:**  
\- *Strengths:* One of the most recognized names in SA auto retail; nationwide network providing convenience; exclusive distribution rights for certain top and mid-tier tire brands; experienced management in automotive retail; high customer loyalty due to service quality. The business model yields steady aftermarket demand.  
\- *Weaknesses:* South Africa-centric – reliant on one country’s economy and car parc (although SA is biggest car market in Africa); retail operations sensitive to rent and utility costs (South Africa’s power issues can affect operations, though they likely have backup power for stores); limited growth rate intrinsically (tire industry grows roughly with vehicle population, which is steady but not high growth).  
\- *Opportunities:* Geographic expansion (maybe deeper into Africa – but franchise or greenfield in other countries can be challenging, still possible in some like Kenya, but no evidence they did under Carlyle; possibly they focused on consolidating SA market share); expanding product lines (e.g., offering more car services beyond tires to capture more wallet share, essentially becoming auto service centers – if not done already); e-commerce and mobile installation units for convenience; and capturing more of the commercial fleet market or mining tires, etc., if not already.  
\- *Threats:* Automotive trends – e.g., if ride-sharing reduces personal car usage, or electric vehicles (EVs) – though EVs still use tires, but might have different maintenance patterns; also any major new competitor (if a global chain tried to enter SA market); economic downturn reducing discretionary parts like fancy alloy wheels (Tiger also sells a lot of mag wheels, which are discretionary – that part goes down in tough times, even if basic tire replacement can’t be avoided forever). Also fuel price and interest rate spikes can reduce driving and new car sales, indirectly affecting the business.

For Alterra, TiAuto was an opportunity to back a **strong brand in consumer retail**. It’s likely provided stable cash flows (which helps the fund’s overall performance). However, unlocking a high multiple exit might be challenging if growth has been modest. They may need a buyer who values the cash generation and market position – possibly a family office or strategic. Regardless, Alterra’s involvement likely helped TiAuto modernize, maybe implement better inventory and customer relationship systems, and think of strategic expansion.

TiAuto’s eventual exit will mark one of the last original Carlyle SSA fund assets to go. Its story highlights Alterra’s engagement in the real economy – tires and car maintenance might not be glamorous, but it’s a backbone sector in consumer life and Alterra’s presence here balanced out riskier tech or financial bets in the fund.

### Traxys

*(Traxys was included in the team deals list primarily due to Carlyle’s involvement, but as a global company it was not an Alterra-managed asset. We provide context for completeness.)*

**Profile:** Traxys is a global commodities **trading and logistics firm** specializing in metals, minerals, and mining products. Headquartered in Luxembourg, Traxys sources, markets, and transports a wide range of commodities (from base metals like copper and zinc to minor metals and ferroalloys) and provides financing and logistical support to miners and processors worldwide. It also had a focus on sourcing from emerging markets, including Africa. By 2014, Traxys had annual turnover over $6 billion[\[184\]](https://www.pehub.com/pe-backed-firms-set-tone-for-european-ipos-carlyle-to-exit-traxys/#:~:text=PE,turnover%20was%20over%20%246). It effectively connects mining output to industrial end-users, often bridging juniors or state-owned miners with international buyers. Traxys also offers marketing off-take agreements, logistics handling, and even project financing for producers. In Africa, Traxys has been active in countries like DRC, South Africa, Mozambique, etc., dealing in minerals like cobalt, tin, tantalum, etc., and ensuring they reach global smelters or manufacturers.

**Investment:** In 2014, Carlyle (with co-investor affiliates of Louis Bacon, founder of Moore Capital) **acquired a majority stake in Traxys**[\[185\]](https://www.connectmoney.com/stories/carlyle-group-louis-bacon-to-sell-metals-merchant-traxys-to-market-maker-optiver/#:~:text=Carlyle%20Group%2C%20Louis%20Bacon%20to,Dutch%20firm)[\[186\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-group-and-louis-bacon-complete-acquisition-traxys-group#:~:text=The%20Carlyle%20Group%20and%20Louis,Company%20and%20Resource%20Capital%20Funds). This was through Carlyle’s broader resources fund, not the Africa fund, but since members of Carlyle’s Africa team had involvement (Traxys’ Africa deals likely received input from Africa team), it’s cited as a team deal. The idea was to leverage Carlyle’s capital to grow Traxys’ trading book and perhaps acquire complementary businesses. Traxys was attractive as it plays in niche metals trading with decent margins and had a strong management team (Traxys management retained stakes). Under Carlyle’s ownership, Traxys expanded its footprint in critical materials (like expanding presence in battery materials sourcing). Given Traxys had operations on multiple continents, it was not “Africa-focused,” but Africa is a big source region for many commodities Traxys trades (e.g., cobalt from DRC, tantalum from Rwanda, etc.).

**Outcome:** Carlyle and Bacon’s affiliates held Traxys until 2023\. **In early 2023, Carlyle agreed to sell its stake in Traxys to Traxys management and Optiver (a Dutch trading firm)**[\[117\]](https://www.pehub.com/carlyle-and-bacon-affiliates-to-exit-traxys-group/#:~:text=The%20Carlyle%20Group%20will%20sell,natural%20resources%20trader%20and%20merchant)[\[96\]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-06/carlyle-to-sell-metals-trader-traxys-to-group-led-by-high-frequency-firm-optiver#:~:text=Carlyle%20to%20Sell%20Metals%20Trader,the%20first%20time%20since). This effectively meant Traxys would be majority-owned by its management team and Optiver (a large market-making and trading company), ending Traxys’ near-decade under PE ownership. The deal was expected to close by May 2023[\[117\]](https://www.pehub.com/carlyle-and-bacon-affiliates-to-exit-traxys-group/#:~:text=The%20Carlyle%20Group%20will%20sell,natural%20resources%20trader%20and%20merchant)[\[96\]](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-06/carlyle-to-sell-metals-trader-traxys-to-group-led-by-high-frequency-firm-optiver#:~:text=Carlyle%20to%20Sell%20Metals%20Trader,the%20first%20time%20since). This exit likely generated a reasonable return as Traxys presumably grew EBITDA in that period, although commodity trading firms’ performance can be volatile year to year. The sale indicates Carlyle exiting at a time when commodity markets were quite robust (2021-22 saw high prices and lots of volatility – good for traders). After exit, Traxys continues independently with the new ownership structure.

**Relevance to Alterra:** Traxys’ inclusion in Alterra’s list mainly shows the breadth of deals the team has touched (even if indirectly). It provided exposure to global commodity flows and insight into mining value chains that can inform other investments (like in logistics or mining services). For instance, Alterra’s team could leverage Traxys knowledge when dealing with J\&J (logistics for mines) or Assala (commodity markets knowledge). Traxys wasn’t managed by Alterra after 2020 (as it wasn’t part of CSSAF), so Alterra didn’t advise Traxys directly – it was Carlyle’s global PE domain. So it’s more of a legacy bragging rights deal for the team.

**Market & Competition:** Traxys competes with big commodity traders like Glencore (huge in base metals), Trafigura, and smaller niche traders. Traxys carved out a niche in technical and minor metals and as a go-between for many mid-size producers the giants might overlook. It also distinguishes by offering financing solutions (like prepayment for offtake).

**SWOT:** (briefly, since Alterra not involved now)  
\- *Strengths:* Deep expertise in metals trading; diversified portfolio of commodities; integrated logistics solutions; a strong risk management platform (necessary in trading).  
\- *Weaknesses:* Thin margins in trading, high reliance on credit lines; exposure to commodity price swings and counterparty risk; being smaller than Glencore limits scale somewhat.  
\- *Opportunities:* Booming demand for critical minerals (battery metals, etc.) plays to Traxys’ strengths; expansion in emerging markets sources; potentially leveraging Optiver’s tech to improve trading.  
\- *Threats:* Global economic downturns reduce commodity volumes; increased regulation on commodity trading; competition from much larger trading houses.

For Alterra’s narrative, Traxys emphasizes their experience in **natural resources and logistics**, adding depth to their resume. Now that Carlyle exited Traxys, Alterra can cite it as a completed success of the broader team. It’s interesting for a GenAI angle too: commodity trading is an area where AI is being applied (market prediction, logistics optimization). But since it’s outside Alterra’s current scope, we won’t delve deep there.

* *Exit Environment Constraints:* Exits in Africa rely heavily on strategic sales, as public markets are shallow. If global strategic interest in Africa wanes (say due to global protectionism or if Africa’s growth story falters), exits might get delayed or come at lower multiples. * *Internal & Operational Risks:* As Alterra scales (managing more assets, possibly launching more funds), maintaining its nimbleness and culture is a challenge. There’s key-man risk: a lot rides on the MDs like Sangudi, Kump, Fort, etc. If any were to depart, it could disrupt operations or LP confidence. Also, operational risk such as compliance failures (given many jurisdictions involved) or an ESG incident in a portfolio company could tarnish Alterra’s reputation given their high ESG stance. **Portfolio-Level Patterns and SWOT Themes:**

Across Alterra’s diverse portfolio, we can glean some common patterns in strengths and weaknesses:

* **Strengths (Portfolio Patterns):** Many of Alterra’s portfolio companies hold **market-leading positions** in their sectors or geographies – e.g., Amrod (promo goods leader)[\[149\]](https://www.carlyle.com/media-room/news-release-archive/carlyle-agrees-to-sell-majority-stake-in-amrod-to-oppenheimer-partners#:~:text=Founded%20in%202000%2C%20Amrod%20has,Amrod%E2%80%99s), CMC (Pan-African network reach)[\[60\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=CMC%20Networks%20operates%20across%20more,the%20Americas%2C%20and%20Asia%20Pacific), NOSA (safety services leader)[\[74\]](https://www.africaprivateequitynews.com/p/carlyle-exits-south-african-company#:~:text=Founded%20in%201952%20as%20NOSA%2C,of%20over%20540%20skilled%20professionals), Tessara (global grape packaging leader)[\[114\]](https://alterracapital.com/#:~:text=). This “invest in \#1 or \#2 player” strategy yields pricing power and resilience. Additionally, most portfolio businesses address **essential needs** (healthcare, food supply, transportation, finance), giving them some demand stability. A focus on **value-add and differentiated products** (not commoditized) is seen – e.g., GCR provides specialized service, Abacus pharma has unique distribution, Traxys had integrated trade logistics, etc. This differentiation is a strength: portfolio companies are not easily displaced by competitors. * **Weaknesses (Portfolio Patterns):** A number of portfolio companies operate in or depend on **challenging operating environments** – for instance, Diamond Bank was exposed to Nigeria’s tough banking climate, J\&J to physical infrastructure limits, Abacus to East African regulatory differences, etc. This can drag performance at times. Some are in **low-margin sectors** requiring scale (e.g., distribution, trading) – requiring constant growth to maintain returns. Another pattern: **dependency on regulatory regimes** – GCR needs supportive capital market regs, NOSA relies on OHS enforcement, Abacus partly relies on health procurement policies. If regulatory priority shifts or enforcement lax, it can impact them. On the portfolio ESG side, while Alterra has improved ESG, some sectors inherently carry environmental/social risk (oil production in Assala, transport emissions in J\&J, chemical use in Tessara). Managing those is critical; any slip could be a portfolio weakness impacting Alterra’s image.

* **Opportunities (Portfolio Patterns):** Many Alterra companies have headroom to **expand regionally** or broaden products – a pattern Alterra has pursued. For example, turning national leaders into regional champions (Amrod into Africa-wide supplier, NOSA into Africa safety brand, Tiger into maybe an Africa tyre chain, etc.). As Africa’s economies formalize, some informal markets become formal (benefiting companies like GCR as more entities need ratings, or like ETG as farming commercializes) – portfolio companies can capitalize on these macro shifts. Cross-portfolio synergies could also be explored: for instance, could GCR’s insights help Alterra assess credit risk for portfolio companies or potential lendings? A synchronized slowdown (like the one caused by COVID-19) can hit nearly all sectors at once – reduced consumer spending (Amrod, Tiger), lower travel (Wakanow), stress on banks (Diamond), etc. The portfolio’s diversity mitigates isolated issues, but not a broad shock. Overall, Alterra’s portfolio strengths outweigh weaknesses in many respects: they have chosen sectors wisely and improved the companies notably. ## GenAI Masterclass Implications for Alterra and Portfolio

Given the above deep-dive into Alterra and its portfolio, we can now tailor implications for a Generative AI (GenAI) masterclass for Alterra’s leadership and portfolio executives. The masterclass should address how GenAI technologies (like large language models, generative image/audio models, etc.) can be leveraged in their specific context, as well as data readiness, risk management, and ethical considerations. Below, we outline key areas and use cases, referencing Alterra’s situation and portfolio to ground the discussion:

**1\. GenAI Use Cases by Industry/Sector:** Alterra’s portfolio spans multiple industries, each with distinct pain points that GenAI could help address:

* *Financial Services (Credit Ratings, Banking):* For **GCR (rating agency)**, GenAI can assist analysts by quickly generating first-draft credit reports, summarizing vast financial datasets, or even predicting rating changes using patterns from historical data (though careful human validation needed)[\[86\]](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthony-njoroge-42a92710_i-am-very-proud-to-share-that-sifem-has-committed-activity-7354210824548245506-ESpi#:~:text=emerging%20markets,rigour%20and%20integrity%2C%20and%20I). A GenAI (like a fine-tuned language model on financial analysis) could automate parts of the rating process, freeing analysts to focus on qualitative judgment. In banking (e.g., Diamond Bank’s context or any fintech Alterra might invest in), GenAI-powered chatbots can improve customer service, handling routine inquiries in local languages 24/7. It can also aid in **fraud detection and credit scoring** by analyzing unstructured data (social media, text descriptions) to find signals of creditworthiness or fraud risk that traditional models miss. For example, a GenAI could parse news and generate early warning summaries about major borrowers or macro conditions that might affect credit risk. This enhances proactive risk management.

* *Healthcare & Pharmaceuticals:* In **Abacus Pharma**, GenAI could optimize supply chain and inventory management. A large language model trained on sales, seasonality, and logistics data can generate demand forecasts or reorder schedules in plain language for managers[\[72\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/deals/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-arp-africa/#:~:text=ARP%20Africa%2C%20which%20includes%20Pollman%E2%80%99s,management%20services%20in%20East%20Africa)[\[120\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/deals/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-arp-africa/#:~:text=global%20visitors%20to%20East%20Africa). It could also assist in training sales reps or pharmacists by generating Q\&A about product information or translating medical content for different markets (English to Swahili, etc.). Over time, as telemedicine grows, GenAI might be used in triaging patient queries or providing basic medical advice, which could tie into pharma distribution (guiding patients to Abacus-supplied medications). Data-wise, Abacus would need to consolidate sales and epidemiological data to feed such models.

* *Industrial/Manufacturing:* In companies like **Cobra Group (mining vehicles)** and potentially **Tessara (packaging)**, GenAI can support design and engineering. For instance, Cobra’s engineers could use generative design algorithms to create new vehicle body variants optimized for different use cases, by setting parameters and letting the AI propose designs (a form of generative AI in CAD). GenAI can also generate maintenance manuals or training guides for technicians in simple language, perhaps even multi-language, increasing workforce effectiveness. On the operations side, an AI model could analyze machinery sensor data and *generate alerts or narratives* about maintenance needs (“Vehicle \#203 likely needs brake replacement in 1000km based on pattern” – a natural language generation from sensor stats). This predictive maintenance keeps uptime high[\[187\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-cobra/#:~:text=Africa%2C%20Australia%2C%20and%20the%20Middle,duty%20hose%20reels%20globally)[\[30\]](https://www.avca.africa/news-insights/member-news/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-cobra/#:~:text=Renschke%20Volschenk%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,%E2%80%9D). Tessara could use AI to simulate and optimize chemical formulations or generate new marketing content for different regions (advertising how its product preserves fruit, tailored to, say, Spanish for Latin American customers or Mandarin for Chinese customers – GenAI can help with fluent, persuasive translation and copywriting).

* *Consumer & Retail:* For **Tiger Wheel & Tyre (TiAuto)** and **Amrod**, GenAI presents many front-office and back-office opportunities. Customer interaction bots can handle appointment bookings (“Chat with TigerBot to schedule your tire change and get tire recommendations based on your car and driving habits”). Amrod’s sales could use AI-driven personalization: the AI generates custom product suggestions or marketing slogans for each corporate client’s campaign, increasing sales conversion. In digital marketing, GenAI can create social media content or product descriptions at scale for Amrod’s 14k SKUs, saving huge time. Internally, these companies can use AI to auto-generate reports (e.g., daily sales summary in plain English each morning for management). E-commerce search could be improved by AI understanding natural language queries (“show me affordable alloy wheels for a Toyota SUV”) and delivering accurate results with generated explanations. Data readiness is moderate here: they have lots of transaction data and product data which, if cleaned, can train recommendation models.

* *Tourism & Travel:* **ARP Travel Group (Pollman’s/Ranger Safaris)** stands to gain from GenAI through enhanced customer experience. A generative AI travel assistant could help customers plan personalized itineraries (“I want a 5-day luxury safari with focus on photography”) by pulling from ARP’s database of tours and creating a tailored itinerary description, including lodge details, animal sightings probabilities, etc. This not only improves pre-sales engagement but also can be used by travel agents as a tool. ARP can also use AI to generate multilingual content to attract non-English speaking tourists (important for Europe/Asia markets). On the operational side, analyzing customer feedback (often unstructured) with AI sentiment analysis can help ARP improve services. Ethically, ARP must ensure content generated (like wildlife descriptions) is accurate and not overpromising – requiring a human in the loop to verify the AI’s creative suggestions.

* *Logistics & Supply Chain:* **J\&J Africa** (though exited) and similar logistics firms can benefit from AI in route optimization and demand forecasting. A GenAI model can take into account weather, border wait times, truck statuses and *generate an optimal dispatch plan each day, explained in natural language* for the operations team (“Given current conditions, Truck A should take route X to avoid congestion, expect 30 min delay at border”). This marries analytical output with human-friendly language[\[188\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=Fahad%20Alhajeri%2C%20CEO%20at%20center3%2C,knowledge%2C%20capability%20in%20the%20African)[\[189\]](https://www.cmcnetworks.com/news/227-center3-acquires-cmc-networks-in-strategic-move-to-accelerate-growth-plan.html#:~:text=footprint%2C%20high%20value%20customer%20base,%E2%80%9D). For current portfolio like possible future investments in logistics, this is applicable. Also in ETG (agri supply chain), AI could help in forecasting crop yields or global price trends and *generate advisories* (“This season’s sesame output may drop 10% due to drought – consider sourcing from backup countries”). Essentially turning data into decision-oriented narratives is GenAI’s strong suit and very useful for busy executives.

**2\. Data Readiness and Requirements:** To exploit these use cases, Alterra’s portfolio companies need to assess their data maturity:

* Many companies have lots of **transactional and structured data** (sales records, inventory, financials). GenAI often needs either training on large external datasets or fine-tuning with internal data. For example, a customer service chatbot needs historical chat/email data to learn from. If that data isn’t collected or is siloed, it must be consolidated. Alterra could encourage each company to invest in data infrastructure: e.g., implementing modern ERPs, CRMs, and IoT sensors to gather data exhaust from operations. An **audit of data assets** is a recommended step. The masterclass can include an exercise for execs: list your data sources (CRM, production logs, social media mentions, etc.), and discuss how clean, accessible, and comprehensive they are. Likely gaps will emerge (maybe Abacus doesn’t have a real-time inventory database – thus an AI can’t forecast stock-outs properly until they implement one). Alterra might consider a portfolio-wide data initiative, perhaps hiring a data science advisor to help companies prepare data for AI projects. This could be pitched to LPs as value-add and impact (developing digital capabilities).

* **Quality and Localization of Data:** African companies may need GenAI that understands local languages, accents, contexts. For instance, if ARP Travel’s chatbot needs to converse with a French speaker for a Chad safari, the AI should ideally know some tourism context in French. Off-the-shelf models (like GPT-4) are strong in major languages but may not know specific local content (like details of a particular Tanzanian park). This means companies might fine-tune models on their own knowledge base (documents, past chats, etc.) to improve accuracy[\[71\]](https://www.africaglobalfunds.com/news/private-equity/deals/alterra-capital-partners-invests-in-arp-africa/#:~:text=Genevieve%20Sangudi%2C%20Partner%20at%20Alterra%2C,%E2%80%9D). Data readiness here means digitizing all that knowledge (maybe ARP’s decades of tour logs or Amrod’s product specs) so AI can learn from it. The masterclass would highlight steps: digitize analog records, unify data formats, ensure privacy compliance (especially with customer data before feeding to AI – e.g., Diamond Bank scenario would involve serious data privacy considerations).

* **Cloud Infrastructure:** To leverage GenAI, companies often need robust cloud computing and data storage solutions. Many GenAI services (Microsoft’s Azure OpenAI, Google’s Vertex AI) are cloud-based. If Alterra’s companies haven’t yet moved data to the cloud, this might be an opportune time (cloud also eases scaling AI experiments). However, there’s the consideration of data residency (some African regulators might require certain data to stay onshore, e.g., in banking). The masterclass should address this: strategies like using on-premise AI solutions vs. cloud, and hybrid approaches.

* **Talent and Skills:** Data readiness isn’t just tech – it’s people. Does each company have data scientists or at least analysts who can work with AI insights? If not, Alterra might help them upskill or even share resources across the portfolio. Perhaps an idea: an Alterra “Data Academy” where a central team assists portfolio companies in implementing pilot AI projects. Without skills, even if data is there, GenAI’s value won’t be realized. So a recommendation to leadership: invest in training your IT and analytics teams on AI tools (maybe get a few staff certified in AI/ML fundamentals).

**3\. Ethical and Risk Considerations:** GenAI brings notable risks, which must be managed carefully to avoid reputational or legal issues:

* *Accuracy & Hallucination:* GenAI models sometimes produce confident-sounding but incorrect information. In a context like credit ratings (GCR) or healthcare (Abacus giving advice), inaccuracies can be dangerous[\[86\]](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/anthony-njoroge-42a92710_i-am-very-proud-to-share-that-sifem-has-committed-activity-7354210824548245506-ESpi#:~:text=emerging%20markets,rigour%20and%20integrity%2C%20and%20I). The class must stress that AI outputs should be verified, especially in critical decisions. Perhaps Alterra companies could implement a “human-in-the-loop” policy: AI provides a draft or recommendation, then a qualified employee reviews and approves. For example, an AI might draft a credit rating rationale, but the lead analyst ensures all facts are correct and nothing material is missed before issuance. Or a chatbot answering medical queries should have a threshold on when to defer to a human (“I am not sure, please consult a pharmacist”). Tools and settings exist to reduce hallucinations (like providing the AI with context documents and instructing it not to deviate; some LLMs allow retrieval augmentation to ground answers in factual data).

* *Bias & Fairness:* AI models can inherit biases from training data. For Alterra’s use cases, consider hiring or credit decisions – if a Diamond Bank used AI to screen loan applicants, it might inadvertently favor or disfavor groups based on biased historical data. Similarly, an AI used in hiring at portfolio companies (some are large employers like Amrod or J\&J) could reflect bias. The masterclass should cover conducting AI bias audits: checking outputs for disparate impact on genders, ethnic groups, etc. Also, ensuring diversity in training data – e.g., if ARP’s chatbot only trained on content about luxury safaris, it might not serve budget travelers well. Alterra’s ESG orientation means they should be proactive: perhaps implementing guidelines that any AI-driven decision that affects people (credit approval, hiring shortlist, etc.) be reviewed for fairness metrics. There are emerging tools to detect and mitigate bias in AI which the class can introduce.

* *Data Privacy & Security:* Many portfolio firms handle sensitive data (NOSA has individuals’ training records and incident reports; GCR has confidential financial info; Diamond Bank had personal financial data). Using AI, especially cloud-based, raises privacy questions. Companies must ensure compliance with laws like POPIA (in South Africa) or NDPR (Nigeria) or GDPR if dealing with EU data. That might mean anonymizing data before feeding to AI, or using self-hosted models for ultra-sensitive info instead of external APIs that might store data. For example, instead of sending actual customer emails to OpenAI API to summarize complaints, maybe use an on-prem open-source model or ensure you have an enterprise arrangement that doesn’t log data. The masterclass should bring up actual policies: e.g., "Do not paste full customer records into ChatGPT," which is a real concern companies globally are addressing. Instead, have IT provide a secure internal AI tool. Alterra could facilitate portfolio-wide adoption of an enterprise AI solution (e.g., Microsoft Azure OpenAI where data is isolated).

* *Job Impact and Change Management:* Executives and employees may worry AI will replace jobs or alter roles significantly. Alterra companies value job creation (some explicitly measure it as impact). The stance likely to be taken: GenAI is there to augment employees, not replace. It can free staff from mundane tasks to focus on higher-value work. But to mitigate fear, Alterra could encourage companies to involve employees in AI adoption, provide re-skilling where needed, and be transparent about any workforce changes. Ethically, given high unemployment in many African countries, large-scale automation has social implications; Alterra being an impact-conscious investor should weigh these. Perhaps they’ll choose to implement AI in ways that enhance productivity without layoffs, using growth to re-balance roles. Or if certain roles become redundant, help transition those employees to new roles (for instance, a call center agent might transition to supervising the AI chatbot and handling complex cases).

* *Trust and Transparency:* For customer-facing AI (e.g., ARP’s travel chatbot or Tiger’s), it’s important to disclose it’s AI if the customer might assume otherwise. Transparency builds trust – e.g., “I am an AI assistant, but I have access to our safari database to help you” should be conveyed to users. Similarly, if AI is used in decision-making (like credit scoring), regulators might in future require explainability. Alterra should get ahead by choosing AI solutions that offer explanation features (some AI models can highlight which inputs influenced the output). The class can discuss XAI (explainable AI) techniques to ensure decisions can be explained in human terms – vital for credit, hiring, etc., both ethically and for compliance.

* *Alignment with ESG goals:* Alterra’s ESG\&I policy should incorporate AI ethics as a new dimension. For example, if job creation is an impact goal, how does automation align? Perhaps by focusing on how AI can allow employees to do more value-added tasks and thus help companies grow and hire more in the long run (the narrative being AI improves competitiveness, leading to expansion and net job growth, not just replacement). They should also consider the environmental impact: large AI models consume a lot of energy – not directly a big issue for limited enterprise use, but something like training a huge model can have a carbon footprint. If any portfolio decides to train its own model, considering green cloud providers or scheduling during off-peak (if they have renewable energy contracts) could align with climate goals.

**4\. Specific Masterclass Modules and Recommendations:**

Given these points, the masterclass can be structured into modules such as: \- **“AI 101 for Business Leaders”:** demystifying GenAI, providing examples relevant to each Alterra sector, making it clear where it can realistically add value vs hype. Use cases from above tailored to Alterra’s portfolio should be highlighted (making it tangible – e.g., “Here’s how a tire retailer in the US used AI to automate scheduling – how might Tiger do something similar?”).  
\- **“Data Foundations”:** stressing data as fuel for AI. Possibly involving an interactive assessment of each company’s data readiness. Could introduce tools for data cleaning and integration – maybe Alterra can partner with a data engineering firm to offer portfolio an assessment.  
\- **“Implementing GenAI Projects”:** guidelines on starting with small pilots (e.g., pick one use case like a chatbot or AI-generated report, test it). Emphasize iterative development, involve cross-functional teams (IT, operations, risk). Possibly share success stories from others (maybe an African bank or telco that deployed an AI solution)[\[190\]](https://www.reuters.com/article/business/carlyles-africa-dealmakers-leave-to-start-their-own-buyout-firm-idUSKBN2331MP/#:~:text=Carlyle%27s%20deals%20in%20Africa%20have,over%20Diamond%20Bank%20last%20year).  
\- **“Ethics, Governance & Risk”:** as elaborated above, covering bias, privacy, oversight, etc. Recommending that companies set up an AI governance committee (maybe part of their existing risk or IT governance) to evaluate AI tools before deployment. Also, ensure compliance with any emerging AI regulations (the EU AI Act potentially affects multinationals; African regulators may follow suit). Alterra could encourage a **portfolio-wide AI ethics charter** – a short document each company signs on to, aligning with Alterra’s values, promising responsible AI use. This could be a differentiator and risk mitigator.  
\- **“Workforce Enablement and Change”:** guiding leaders on how to bring employees along. Suggest training programs for staff on using AI tools (for example, train marketing teams to use ChatGPT for first drafts but also how to critically edit AI output). Encourage an innovation culture where employees are incentivized to identify AI opportunities (maybe an internal competition for best AI idea in each company). Address anxieties openly.

**5\. Role for Alterra (Masterclass Outcome):** The masterclass isn’t just theoretical – it should equip Alterra and portfolio executives with an action plan. Potential outcomes: \- Each company identifies 1-2 pilot AI initiatives to pursue in the next 6 months, along with data prep needed. They would also identify any support needed (maybe Group-level help or external vendors – Alterra might negotiate group rates with an AI solutions provider to assist portfolio companies). \- Alterra might consider creating a **central AI task force** or hiring a Chief Technology Advisor who can travel across companies to guide implementations. This is feasible with their fund size and would directly support value creation (just as some PEs have operating partners for digital transformation). \- Emphasize measuring the impact: ensure that for each AI use case, companies define KPIs (like time saved, cost reduced, sales increased) and also monitor for any negative signals (like customer satisfaction changes if using AI chatbot). Alterra could incorporate these metrics into its regular portfolio review process.

**6\. Risk/Ethics Focus Recap:** Summarizing for leaders: \- Always maintain a human veto on critical AI-driven decisions (don’t fully cede decisions that can significantly harm individuals without review). \- Protect your data and customer trust – anonymize, secure, comply with laws. \- Be transparent – if using AI in customer interactions or data usage, lean towards openness. \- Start small and safe – internal-facing AI tools (like report generation) pose less risk than customer-facing ones, so maybe do those first to build confidence. \- Upskill your people so AI is a tool in their hand, not a mysterious box.

By the end of the masterclass, Alterra’s leadership should have a clear view that GenAI, when thoughtfully applied, can accelerate the same value creation they seek: improving efficiency, scaling personalization, and unlocking new growth avenues – all while needing solid data and oversight. The message is: **GenAI is a powerful new lever in the toolkit, but it must be pulled carefully and deliberately**. With Alterra’s emphasis on sustainability and long-term growth, they can become pioneers in **responsible AI adoption in African private equity**, setting standards for others. This would not only benefit portfolio performance but also enhance Alterra’s reputation as forward-looking and principled – a nice alignment with their brand and values.
